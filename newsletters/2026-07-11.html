Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

As agentic AI takes on more of the mechanics of financial analysis, the concepts your users work with daily become your product's inputs, outputs, and risk surface. Six building blocks that every AI PM in this space needs to have working fluency with — and that come up constantly in analyst and portfolio manager conversations.

Short Selling
Borrowing shares to sell now and buy back later at a lower price — a bet on decline. Shorts create unique data signals (short interest, borrow cost, squeeze risk) that AI sentiment tools increasingly need to surface alongside long positioning.
Yield Curve
A plot of bond yields across different maturities (e.g. 2-year vs. 10-year). Its shape signals economic expectations. An inverted yield curve (short rates above long rates) has historically preceded recessions — a key macro signal for AI models to interpret.
Drawdown
The percentage decline from a portfolio's peak value to its trough before recovery. Maximum drawdown is a standard risk metric for fund managers. AI risk tools that fail to flag rising drawdown risk before it is visible in returns are missing their primary use case.
Duration Risk
The sensitivity of a bond's price to interest rate changes. Longer-dated bonds have higher duration — they fall more when rates rise. AI products that support fixed-income portfolio management must correctly model duration to give analysts useful risk estimates.
Credit Spread
The yield difference between a corporate bond and an equivalent government bond. Wider spreads signal more perceived credit risk. AI tools monitoring spreads in real time give fixed-income analysts an early warning system for credit stress across a portfolio.
Basis Points (bps)
A unit of measurement equal to 0.01% (one hundredth of a percent). Used universally for interest rates, yield changes, and fee comparisons. When a portfolio manager says "spreads widened 25 bps," they mean 0.25%. This shorthand appears in every analyst conversation.

MAS Publishes SAFR — The Most Detailed Governance Framework for AI Agents in Finance Yet

The Monetary Authority of Singapore published the SAFR (Safeguards for Agentic Finance at Runtime) white paper on July 3, developed with Mastercard, Visa, OCBC, Manulife, Ant International, and others. SAFR specifies four technical safeguards: policy-bound execution, real-time validation, full auditability, and interoperability — applied to payments, treasury, wealth advisory, and client engagement workflows.

Why It Matters

SAFR is the blueprint your engineers need to read before designing any agentic workflow that touches financial transactions. Its four safeguards — especially policy-bound execution and auditability — are the exact design constraints your PRD should already specify. If they're not in your spec, they need to be.

Research & Think About
  • Download the SAFR white paper PDF at mas.gov.sg — it is concise, technically specific, and the most useful governance document published by any financial regulator on agentic AI this year
  • Research what "policy-bound execution" means in practice: an agent that can only act within pre-defined rules, with every deviation flagged for human review — this is the model for safe agentic deployment in regulated environments
  • For one agentic workflow in your product roadmap, map each of SAFR's four safeguards: where does your current design satisfy them, and where does it fall short?

FCA · Mills Review · July 6

FCA Publishes Landmark Mills Review: Seven Recommendations to Govern AI in Retail Finance

The FCA's Mills Review — published July 6 — is the UK's most comprehensive official assessment of AI in financial services. Seven recommendations include: expand the regulatory perimeter to cover AI model providers, scale up the FCA's AI Lab, enable "agentic finance" infrastructure, and build an AI-enabled supervisory model. Critically, the review recommends no new AI-specific rules — the existing principles-based framework stays. One in five UK adults (11M people) are likely to use autonomous AI agents for financial decisions.

Why It Matters

The principles-based framework staying intact is good news for your product — but "agentic finance" being named a regulatory priority means every autonomous workflow you build now has a regulatory track running alongside it. The FCA is telling you where it's looking; design accordingly.

Research & Think About
  • Read the full Mills Review at fca.org.uk/publications/corporate-documents/mills-review — Recommendation 5 ("Enable the foundations for agentic finance") is the one most directly relevant to your product roadmap
  • Research what "expanding the regulatory perimeter" to cover AI model providers would mean in practice — which of your current AI vendors (Anthropic, OpenAI, others) would fall under FCA supervision?
  • Draft a one-paragraph "regulatory risk section" for your next PRD: which FCA principles (Consumer Duty, SM&CR, operational resilience) are most likely to apply to each AI feature you're shipping?

Anthropic Redeploys Claude Fable 5 After US Export-Control Order Lifted

On July 1, Anthropic redeployed Claude Fable 5 — its Mythos-class flagship — after the US government lifted a June 12 export-control order that had pulled the model offline for nearly three weeks. The control order had restricted global deployment pending national security review; its lifting signals that Fable 5 cleared the same review pathway GPT-5.6 subsequently passed. Why It Matters

Government export controls on frontier AI models are now a real operational risk for any product built on a single model provider. A three-week model outage would be catastrophic for analyst workflows — your architecture needs to account for this. Model-agnostic design is no longer just a vendor-lock-in concern.

Research & Think About

How Product Is Changing in 2026: AI Takes the Grunt Work, Humans Own the Ambiguity

Ant Murphy's widely-shared 2026 piece argues AI is absorbing product management's administrative and analytical layer — requirements documentation, market sizing, data synthesis — leaving PMs to own what AI cannot: resolving ambiguity, building stakeholder trust, and making judgment calls under uncertainty. The PMs struggling are those whose value was in the tasks AI now does faster.

Why It Matters

In a financial services context, "resolving ambiguity" means navigating between what analysts say they want and what they actually need — a skill that requires domain knowledge AI cannot replicate. Your edge is not in doing PM tasks faster — it's in understanding the problem space more deeply than any tool can.

Research & Think About
  • Read Ant Murphy's full piece at antmurphy.medium.com — the distinction between "task-level PM" and "judgment-level PM" is the most useful framing for understanding where to invest your development time in 2026
