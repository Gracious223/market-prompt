Talent & Competitive Risk

Google's AI Brain Drain: Four Top Researchers Left for Rivals in Six Days

Google's AI division lost at least four senior researchers to competitors in less than a week. On June 18, Noam Shazeer — a co-author of the original "Attention Is All You Need" paper that all modern AI is built on — left for OpenAI. Nobel Prize laureate John Jumper, who won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold (an AI that maps the 3D shape of proteins, unlocking drug discovery at scale), announced he was joining Anthropic. This week, Bloomberg confirmed that AlphaFold contributors Jonas Adler and Alexander Pritzel — both key members of Google's Gemini model team — are also leaving for Anthropic. A fifth researcher, Arthur Conmy, is also reported to be departing.

Why this matters to you: Where the most credentialed researchers go is a leading indicator of where the next major AI breakthrough — and the next product advantage — is likely to come from. For Alphabet shareholders (the company behind Google), losing five names of this caliber inside one week is a signal worth monitoring as closely as an earnings revision. It also tells you something about Anthropic's IPO equity: it has now become the most powerful recruiting tool in the industry.

Regulation

Europe Gives AI Companies 16 Extra Months to Meet Its Toughest Compliance Rules

The European Parliament voted 423 to 57 on June 16 to extend the EU AI Act's strictest deadline by 16 months. Companies using AI in "high-risk" applications — which include hiring, credit scoring, law enforcement screening, and university admissions — now have until December 2027 to comply, instead of August 2026. A separate 12-month extension applies to AI embedded in regulated physical products like medical devices and vehicles. The EU Council (the bloc's national governments) is expected to formally sign off before the original August 2026 deadline.

Why this matters to you: This is near-term relief for any company selling AI software into Europe — including Microsoft, SAP, Salesforce, and the fintech platforms most investment banks use. It does not remove the obligation; it moves the deadline. AI product managers should treat this window as an opportunity to build compliance documentation now, not as a reason to deprioritize it — firms that have audit trails ready will have a competitive advantage when the deadline arrives.

AI-Native Finance

Meta Is Building an AI Prediction Market App — and Llama Will Resolve the Bets

Internal Meta documents obtained by NPR reveal a project called "Arena" (codenamed "FBForecast") — a standalone app where Meta's own Llama AI (its large language model, the AI it has open-sourced for developers) automatically generates questions from trending news, makes personalized market recommendations to users, and resolves outcomes in near real-time. Users receive daily virtual play-money — no real-cash wagering at launch — while Meta pursues the regulatory licenses needed to run real-money markets. The app is designed to compete directly with Kalshi and Polymarket, the leading AI-era prediction market platforms.

Why this matters to you: Meta entering prediction markets is a structural threat to specialized platforms that have spent years building audience and liquidity. It also marks a meaningful shift: AI acting as the umpire of real-world event bets is a new category of AI decision-making risk. For investors, it's a signal that AI-native financial products are moving from niche startups into the consumer apps hundreds of millions of people already open every day.

AI Infrastructure

Nvidia's Next-Gen AI Chip Is in Full Production — Nearly Two Quarters Ahead of Schedule

Nvidia's Vera Rubin NVL72 chip — the successor to its Blackwell line — entered full production in Q1 2026, roughly two quarters earlier than the original planned timeline. The chip delivers five times faster AI inference (the step where an AI model generates its answer) and 3.5 times faster AI training than Blackwell. AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, and CoreWeave are among the first cloud providers scheduled to offer it to customers in the second half of 2026. Nvidia says early customers include Anthropic and OpenAI.

Why this matters to you: Earlier-than-expected production shifts the timeline for Nvidia's next earnings catalyst and puts new competitive pressure on AMD and Intel. For teams buying AI infrastructure or building AI cost models, it also signals that the market rate for compute will keep falling — which means any business case relying on today's GPU (graphics processing unit, the specialized chips used to run AI) prices will need a refresh sooner than expected.

From the Labs

AI Safety Research

Anthropic Publishes New Research on Catching AI Systems That Deliberately Underperform

Anthropic's alignment team (the researchers dedicated to making AI safe and predictable) published a new paper this month called "Diffuse AI Control on Fuzzy Tasks." It introduces a red-teaming framework — a structured test designed to catch an AI that is sandbagging (deliberately pretending to be less capable than it is, particularly during safety evaluations). This is the first new post on the Anthropic Alignment Science blog since May, which also produced SLEIGHT-Bench (a tool for finding blind spots in AI monitors) and a study on how safety training generalizes across different tasks.

Why this matters to you: Sandbagging is one of the hardest AI safety problems to detect — a model that hides capabilities during testing while behaving differently in production is especially dangerous in regulated settings like finance, where human auditors must be able to verify what a system is actually doing. Firms deploying AI agents should track this research: it is where the next generation of AI audit requirements is likely to originate.

Governance & Scale

New WEF Report: Financial Firms Have Scaled AI — But Governance Is Now the Bottleneck

The World Economic Forum's June 2026 report on AI in financial services found that 65% of financial institutions are now actively using AI, and nearly 90% are deploying or evaluating it. But governance, trust, and human oversight have emerged as the single biggest barrier to further scale. A parallel Nvidia survey found that one in three financial AI deployments is being slowed by a lack of explainability tools — systems that can show auditors and regulators exactly how an AI reached its conclusion, step by step.

Why this matters to you: The governance gap is becoming a procurement category in its own right, and a potential examination finding (see Watch Closely, below). If your firm is scaling AI faster than its oversight infrastructure, you are building compliance liability, not just capability. The firms that will win the next phase are those that treat explainability and audit trails as product requirements from day one — not retrofits.

Bank Regulation

U.S. Bank Regulators Have Started Grading AI Governance in Routine Examinations

The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (the federal regulator that supervises national banks) and the Federal Reserve have begun embedding AI governance questions into their standard examination programs. Examiners are now asking banks to document AI model guardrails, human oversight procedures, third-party vendor risks, and contingency plans — specifically for AI used in lending decisions, KYC (know-your-customer: the process banks use to verify a client's identity and flag suspicious activity), and sanctions screening. Institutions that can't produce documentation face formal examination findings.

Why this matters to you: AI governance is no longer a best-practice aspiration for U.S. banks — it is now a supervisory exam item with the same weight as capital or liquidity requirements. If you are building AI products for or within U.S. financial institutions, plan to have compliance documentation, audit logs, and human-checkpoint architecture ready before your first pilot goes live, not after your first regulatory conversation.
