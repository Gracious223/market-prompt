Public Markets

AI Infrastructure

Micron Crosses $1 Trillion as the AI Memory Boom Mints Its Newest Giant

Micron, which makes the memory chips that let AI systems store and retrieve data quickly, has become the latest company worth more than $1 trillion in market value (the total worth of all its shares) — overtaking JPMorgan Chase for the first time. The move is part of a wider memory-chip rally: rival chipmaker SanDisk's stock is up roughly 700% so far this year, as AI data centers buy memory faster than factories can produce it.

Why this matters to you: The AI boom's biggest stock-market winners this year aren't the chatbot companies — they're the unglamorous chipmakers supplying the physical hardware underneath them. If you're costing out an AI product internally, remember hardware scarcity, not just software licensing, is what's setting the price.

Funding & Valuation

Anthropic Quietly Files to Go Public at a $965 Billion Price Tag

Anthropic, the company behind the Claude chatbot, confidentially filed paperwork with U.S. regulators on June 1 for an IPO (initial public offering — the first time a company sells shares to ordinary investors on a stock exchange). The filing follows a $65 billion funding round that valued the company at $965 billion, briefly making it more valuable than rival OpenAI. Bankers are targeting an October listing on the Nasdaq stock exchange.

Why this matters to you: When the company behind the AI model your product might run on goes public, its quarterly earnings calls become required reading — they'll tell you, before anyone else does, whether building on frontier AI is getting cheaper or more expensive.

Tools for Analysts & PMs

Research Tools

Bloomberg Puts an AI Agent Inside Every Analyst's Terminal

Bloomberg is rolling out ASKB, a conversational AI tool built into its Terminal (the data and trading platform most professional investors use all day). Instead of typing search commands, analysts can ask plain-language questions, and a coordinated team of AI agents — small AI programs each handling one step of the job — pulls company filings, earnings calls, and news to assemble an answer in minutes.

Why this matters to you: Bloomberg owns the desktop your analysts already live in, which makes ASKB the bar any internal tool you build gets measured against. Worth stealing: it routes easy questions to cheap, fast AI models and saves the expensive ones for hard questions.

Adoption

Seven in Ten Investment Firms Now Use AI Daily — Up From One in Ten

A study of 200 executives at asset managers, pension funds, and insurers, commissioned by financial software firm SimCorp, found 70% are now prominently using AI to support the front office (the staff who manage client money directly) — up from about 10% a year earlier. Vendor stability, not raw model performance, is now the top factor firms weigh when picking an AI tool.

Why this matters to you: Your internal users have gone, in one year, from curious to expectant. "Vendor stability" beating performance as the top selection criterion is a signal that reliability and long-term support now sell better than a flashier model.

From the Labs

Model Release

Microsoft Ships Its Own AI Models to Cut Its OpenAI Dependence

Microsoft unveiled MAI-Thinking-1, its first in-house reasoning model (an AI trained to work through multi-step problems rather than just answer quickly), alongside a coding model called MAI-Code-1-Flash. Both were built without OpenAI — the company Microsoft has spent billions backing — a sign Microsoft wants options beyond its biggest AI partner.

Why this matters to you: Even Microsoft, OpenAI's largest backer, is hedging against depending on one AI supplier. If a company with that much leverage still wants a backup model, your own product roadmap probably shouldn't hard-wire itself to a single AI provider either.

Enterprise AI

Anthropic Launches Ready-Made AI Agents for Banks and Asset Managers

Anthropic released a set of pre-built Claude agents (AI assistants configured for one specific job) aimed at financial firms, covering tasks like investment research and compliance checks. They ship as plugins inside Claude's existing developer tools, so firms don't have to build agent infrastructure from scratch.

Why this matters to you: Anthropic just became a more direct competitor to the in-house tools teams like yours build. The honest question to ask: does your roadmap add something a pre-built agent can't, or are you rebuilding a feature Anthropic now ships for free?

AI Product Management

Skills

Strategy, Not Coding, Is the Skill PMs Say Will Matter Most

A 2026 product-management survey found 59% of product managers (PMs — the people who decide what a company builds and why) now rank strategy and business judgement as the most important skill for the next two to three years, ahead of technical or AI-specific skills. The reasoning: AI is increasingly doing the grunt work of writing specs and crunching data, leaving direction-setting as the harder, more valuable job.

Why this matters to you: As AI absorbs more day-to-day execution work, the part of your job that's hardest to automate — deciding what's worth building at all — is becoming the part that defines whether you're seen as essential.

Watch Closely

UK Regulation

FCA Chief: "Legislation Will Never Keep Up" With AI

Speaking at a financial-services AI conference on June 24, Nikhil Rathi — head of the UK's Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), the regulator that oversees most UK financial firms — said AI is moving too fast for traditional rule-making, and that the FCA will keep leaning on its existing rules rather than writing AI-specific ones. He warned firms not to mistake the absence of new rules for an absence of scrutiny.

Why this matters to you: Regulators worldwide are converging on the same approach: using existing rules like "treat customers fairly" instead of AI-specific law. That puts the burden on you to prove your AI product meets old standards, since there's no new checklist to follow.
