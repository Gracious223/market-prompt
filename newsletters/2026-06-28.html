Public Markets

AI Spending

Companies Are Hitting the AI Brakes After Blowing Their Budgets

For two years, corporations competed to burn through AI as fast as possible — a practice that spread so widely it earned its own name: tokenmaxxing (using AI tokens, the small units of text that AI services charge by, as aggressively as possible, tracked on internal leaderboards as a sign of innovation). The bills have now arrived. Uber exhausted its entire annual budget for Claude Code (Anthropic's AI coding assistant) in just four months, then capped spending at $1,500 per month per user. Amazon shut down its internal AI usage leaderboard. Microsoft revoked corporate Claude Code licenses. AT&T and Meta both tightened AI spending simultaneously. Some companies are switching entirely to cheaper alternatives like DeepSeek (a lower-cost, open-weight Chinese AI model) and reporting similar results at a fraction of the price.

Why this matters to you: This spending correction is a direct headwind for OpenAI and Anthropic — both preparing for IPOs (first-time public stock offerings) in late 2026 and early 2027. If their most enthusiastic customers are cutting consumption and defecting to cheaper models, the revenue growth story those IPOs will be built on becomes harder to tell. Watch Microsoft's and Alphabet's Q2 earnings calls for the first public data on whether AI usage is genuinely growing or plateauing.

AI Infrastructure

Nvidia's Next-Generation AI Chip Is Now in Production — and It Promises to Cut AI Costs by 10x

Nvidia has moved its Vera Rubin AI platform into full manufacturing. The company claims it delivers up to 10 times lower cost per AI token compared to its current Blackwell chips — meaning the same AI query could cost a tenth as much to run — and trains the largest AI models using one-quarter as many chips. AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, and Oracle are all lined up to offer Vera Rubin-based cloud instances (rentable computing power) starting in the second half of 2026, with Dell, HPE, Cisco, and Lenovo manufacturing the servers.

Why this matters to you: If Nvidia's cost claims hold, the economics of AI products shift meaningfully before the end of 2026 — cheaper inference (running AI queries) makes always-on, high-volume AI features viable for a much wider set of business cases. Model this into any roadmap or business case you're writing for the next 12–18 months.

Tools for Analysts & PMs

AI Governance

Every U.S. Bank Audit Now Comes With an AI Questionnaire

The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (the main U.S. regulator for national banks), the Federal Reserve, and the FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, which insures bank deposits) have begun embedding AI-specific questions into every routine bank examination. Examiners are asking banks to map out how AI is used in lending, know-your-customer checks (verifying who a customer is and flagging suspicious activity), and sanctions screening; whether banks have kill switches (the ability to instantly shut an AI system down if something goes wrong); and how they ensure that outside AI vendors meet the same governance standards as the bank itself. A June survey found 72% of banks said they were least prepared on kill-switch protocols. The three agencies plan to issue a formal request for information on bank AI use — including agentic AI (systems that take autonomous actions without human approval at every step) — in the near future.

Why this matters to you: If you are building or deploying AI inside a bank or for a bank client, your product now needs a kill switch, a vendor governance paper trail, and an audit-ready control framework — not as nice-to-haves, but as examination requirements. Build that compliance layer into the product spec from day one.

From the Labs

Research

80% of Financial Firms Are Using AI — But Most Cannot Prove It Is Working

The Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance (CCAF), working with the Bank for International Settlements, the IMF, and the World Economic Forum, published its 2026 Global AI in Financial Services report, covering data from firms and regulators in 149 countries. Key findings: more than 80% of financial services firms are adopting AI, and 52% are already experimenting with agentic AI (systems that take autonomous actions — like executing trades or filing documents — without human sign-off at each step). But 55% of firms say they cannot measure the value of what they are deploying, a figure that rises to 76% among large financial institutions. Meanwhile, only 18% of financial regulators globally have actually scaled AI in their own operations; 48% are still in the exploration phase. No new posts have appeared on Anthropic's alignment science blog since yesterday's coverage of "Diffuse AI Control on Fuzzy Tasks."

Why this matters to you: The gap between industry adoption (fast, widespread) and regulatory oversight (slow, exploratory) is a structural risk. Firms building AI now into regulated financial workflows are operating ahead of the rules — which means the compliance requirements will be written around whatever the early movers built. Getting governance in place before regulation catches up is a competitive advantage, not just a legal box to check.

Market Dynamics

ChatGPT's Share of the AI Market Has Fallen From 76.5% to 54.7% — While Google Gemini Nearly Doubled

Eighteen months ago, ChatGPT captured nearly 77% of all worldwide web traffic across the major AI assistants. As of late June 2026, that share has dropped to 54.7% — still the largest, but a sharp reversal. Google Gemini has climbed to 27.4%, roughly doubling its share in six months, making it the fastest-growing large AI assistant by web traffic. The shift tracks with Google's moves: Gemini integrated into Workspace for three billion users, aggressive pricing cuts, and new platform partnerships that give Gemini reach OpenAI currently lacks.

Why this matters to you: In 2024, choosing which AI to build on was essentially a default — use OpenAI or fall behind. In mid-2026, Google Gemini is a credible primary choice. The platform you build on now shapes your switching costs, your pricing exposure, and your access to new capabilities for years. If your product strategy still treats OpenAI as the only option, that assumption is overdue for a review.

Watch Closely

Regulation

The SEC Is Now Charging Investment Firms for Lying About How They Use AI

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission's 2026 examination priorities place significant focus on "AI washing" — overstating how much or how well a firm uses AI in marketing materials or regulatory filings. In 2024 the SEC charged two investment advisers (firms that manage money for clients and must register with regulators) for misleading investors about their AI capabilities; that precedent now sets the bar for every registered firm. Smaller advisers managing under $1.5 billion in assets faced a June 3, 2026 compliance deadline under updated Regulation S-P (data privacy and safeguards rules). Form ADV Part 2A — the standard disclosure document all advisers file — must now accurately describe whether and how AI is used in portfolio management, research, or client communications, without overstating what the AI can or cannot do.

Why this matters to you: If your firm's marketing, pitch decks, or SEC filings describe AI capabilities that have not been validated, you are now in enforcement territory. The June 3 deadline has passed — if your firm missed it, escalate to legal this week rather than next.

IPO Watch

OpenAI Is Now Weighing a 2027 Delay as the Tokenmaxxing Correction Clouds Its Revenue Story

Reuters reported in late June that OpenAI is now considering pushing its IPO (initial public offering — the first time a private company sells shares to the public) to 2027, after originally targeting late 2026. The consideration follows a shift in sentiment among enterprise customers, who are cutting AI spending and, in some cases, moving workloads to cheaper competitors. Anthropic, which filed its confidential IPO paperwork on June 1 at a reported $965 billion valuation, is still targeting a December 2026 listing, but faces the same headwind: investors will want to see durable revenue growth, not a tokenmaxxing peak followed by a pullback.

Why this matters to you: When OpenAI and Anthropic go public, their quarterly earnings calls become essential reading — they will be the most transparent window into whether AI infrastructure costs are falling, whether enterprise adoption is deepening, and whether the capabilities roadmap you depend on is on track. The timing of those IPOs now depends partly on whether the spending correction stabilizes before year end.
