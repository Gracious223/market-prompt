Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

As AI agents begin operating autonomously in markets — and as you build products that support investment decisions — fluency with the mechanics your users navigate every day sharpens your product thinking and earns credibility in discovery conversations. These six concepts govern how analysts and portfolio managers evaluate positions and manage risk.

Short Selling
Borrowing shares and selling them now, betting the price falls so you can buy back cheaper and return them at a profit. AI tools identifying overvalued securities are enabling more systematic short strategies at scale.
Yield Curve
A graph of bond yields across maturities (2yr to 30yr). Its shape signals economic outlook — an "inverted" curve (short rates above long rates) has historically preceded recessions. Macro AI tools model yield curve dynamics continuously.
Duration
A bond's sensitivity to interest rate changes. A bond with 7-year duration falls roughly 7% if rates rise 1%. AI-powered fixed-income tools model portfolio-level duration exposure in real time — critical for risk teams.
Drawdown
The peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value over a given period. A "maximum drawdown" of 20% means the portfolio fell 20% from its high before recovering. Risk AI tools monitor drawdown limits and alert PMs before mandate breaches.
Earnings Revision
When sell-side analysts raise or lower their earnings estimates for a company. Upward revisions are a bullish signal; downward are bearish. AI tracking revision momentum across thousands of stocks simultaneously is among the highest-demand buy-side product categories.
Systematic vs. Discretionary
Systematic (quant) strategies follow rules and models. Discretionary strategies rely on human judgement and fundamental research. Most leading funds now blend both — and AI is the infrastructure enabling that hybrid approach at scale.

Systemic Risk · BoE

Bank of England Floats Market-Wide Kill Switch for AI Trading Agents

Deputy Governor Sarah Breeden, speaking at the ECB forum in Sintra on July 1, called for purpose-built regulatory frameworks for agentic AI in finance — including market-wide "kill switches" to halt trading if faulty models trigger a meltdown. The core concern: 52% of finance firms already run autonomous agents, and models trained on similar data optimising for similar objectives will respond to the same market signal with the same action at the same time — amplifying volatility rather than absorbing it.

Why It Matters

"Herding behaviour" is a systems-level product problem, not just a trading one. If your agents surface the same signals as every competitor's agents, you amplify market volatility rather than provide edge. Differentiated data and differentiated reasoning are your only hedge against this.

Research & Think About
  • Read Computer Weekly's coverage of Breeden's speech — "herding behaviour" is critical vocabulary for your next PRD risk section
  • Research "model homogeneity" as a systemic risk concept — the BIS has published relevant working papers on correlated AI behaviour in markets
  • Draft: if your product were used by 100 institutions simultaneously acting on the same signals, what would happen to the edges your users are trying to capture?

FCA · Agentic AI

FCA Calls Agentic AI a "Profound Step Change" — Warns Banks on Governance and Accountability Gaps

At techUK's "Agents of Change: Generative and Agentic AI in Financial Services 2026" event, FCA Chief Executive Nikhil Rathi described agentic systems as drivers of a "profound step change" in consumer financial services. Banks conducting live agentic AI trials are exposing consumers to new risks as autonomous agents make decisions without adequate governance frameworks — and the FCA is unequivocal that firms remain fully accountable for every action an agent takes on their behalf.

Why It Matters

"Firms remain accountable" means your product's agent actions are your firm's regulatory liability, full stop. An audit trail, clear escalation path, and human override capacity in every agentic workflow are not design options — they are regulatory requirements in a UK-regulated environment.

Research & Think About
  • Read the Burges Salmon analysis of recent FCA messaging on the "agentic step change" — the most precise synthesis of what regulators are actually watching for in live trials
  • Research what "operational accountability" means under the FCA's SM&CR when AI agents are in the decision loop — who is the named accountable senior manager?
  • Draft a one-paragraph "accountability map" for your product: for each agent action, who is the named accountable human and what is their oversight mechanism?

Consumer Protection · FCA

"Nobody Licensed AI to Give Financial Advice" — 28 Million UK Adults Used It Anyway

Lloyds Banking Group's Consumer Digital Index found that 56% of UK adults — roughly 28 million people — used AI for financial questions in the past 12 months. Financial advice is a regulated activity; the consumer protections covering licensed advisers do not extend to ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. FCA CEO Nikhil Rathi: "Technology is moving much faster than many regulatory paradigms." AI also owes no fiduciary duty to users.

Why It Matters

The gap between what consumers do and what the law protects creates a product design obligation for internal tools: your analysts need to know precisely where AI output ends and regulated advice begins — and your product must make that boundary explicit and visible.

Research & Think About
  • Read PYMNTS's "Nobody Licensed AI to Give Financial Advice" — it frames the fiduciary gap with precision and cites the specific regulations in play
  • Research "fiduciary duty" in a financial context: who owes it, to whom, and what it requires — then map those obligations to your product's current output design
  • Ask your legal team: what disclaimers or output constraints are required when AI outputs could directly inform an investment decision at your firm?

Beyond Prompt Engineering: "Intent Engineering" Is the New Core Skill for AI PMs Building Agentic Products

The 2026 AI PM Roadmap from Product Compass identifies three layers of agent engineering now essential for product leaders: prompt engineering (steering a single answer), context engineering (giving an agent memory and tools), and intent engineering (constraining what an agent that acts autonomously is permitted to do). As products shift from chat interfaces to agentic workflows, PMs who cannot specify agent intent — not just agent instructions — are operating at the wrong level of abstraction.

Why It Matters

"Intent engineering" is what separates a PM who ships agent features from one who ships safe, auditable, bounded agent features that survive a compliance and legal review. In financial services, the inability to constrain and document agent intent is a regulatory blocker, not a product quality issue.

Research & Think About
  • Read the Product Compass AI PM Roadmap 2026 at productcompass.pm — the three-layer engineering framework is the most actionable PM framing of agentic product design currently available
  • Practice intent engineering on your current product: write a one-paragraph "agent constitution" — what is your agent permitted to do, prohibited from doing, and required to escalate to a human?
  • Research Anthropic's Constitutional AI approach as a starting reference, then adapt its principles to the constraints of a regulated financial workflow
