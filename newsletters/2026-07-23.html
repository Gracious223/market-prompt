Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

Today's edition covers fund structures, valuation multiples, and risk metrics your analysts use when evaluating whether a company is cheaply or expensively priced, and how portfolios are categorised by mandate. These terms come up constantly in buy-side conversations — especially when discussing why a fund holds what it holds.

Hedge Fund vs. Long-Only
Hedge funds take both long and short positions, use leverage, and target absolute returns regardless of market direction. Long-only funds (most pension and mutual funds) only buy assets, targeting returns relative to a benchmark. Your users likely span both — product design differs meaningfully.
Net Asset Value (NAV)
Total fund assets minus liabilities, divided by shares outstanding — the per-share value of a fund's portfolio. Mutual funds price at NAV once daily; ETFs trade continuously at market price. AI tools are being used to estimate real-time NAV for complex multi-asset portfolios.
Active vs. Passive Management
Active managers pick individual securities to beat a benchmark; passive funds simply track an index. The shift toward passive (index funds, ETFs) has squeezed active management fees — making the hunt for AI-assisted alpha more commercially urgent than ever.
Drawdown
Peak-to-trough decline in a portfolio's value over a period. A 20% drawdown means the portfolio fell 20% from its high. AI risk tools model maximum drawdown scenarios before they happen — giving portfolio managers visibility into tail risks that historical models miss.
Earnings Per Share (EPS)
Net income divided by shares outstanding — a core measure of company profitability. AI analyst tools are trained to extract and cross-validate EPS figures from earnings transcripts and filings at scale, flagging discrepancies between reported and adjusted figures automatically.
P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
Stock price divided by earnings per share — the most widely used equity valuation multiple. A high P/E implies the market expects growth; a low P/E implies value or risk. AI tools scanning earnings calls now flag unusual P/E divergences across sectors before human analysts catch them.
