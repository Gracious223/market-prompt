Public Markets

Federal Reserve · Rates

Fed Holds Rates Steady, but a Hawkish Dot Plot Under New Chair Warsh Sends Stocks on a Wild Ride

The Federal Reserve held its benchmark rate steady at its June 17 meeting — the first under new Chair Kevin Warsh — but the accompanying dot plot (the anonymous chart showing where each policymaker expects rates to go) rattled markets. Nine of eighteen officials now project at least one more hike this cycle, and the median projection for end-2026 rates climbed to 3.8%, up from 3.4% in March. Warsh himself abstained from submitting a dot, an unusual move for a sitting chair. Equities whipsawed on the news before the Nasdaq closed up roughly 2% by week's end, and the S&P 500 logged its 11th winning week in the last 12.

A higher projected rate path raises the discount rate (the interest rate used to translate a company's future cash flows into today's dollars) used to value long-duration growth stocks — exactly the category most AI companies, and the IPO candidates queued up behind SpaceX, fall into. Every basis point added to the Fed's dot plot makes it that much harder to justify a 2030-cash-flow story for Anthropic or OpenAI at IPO — model your valuation sensitivities accordingly.

Buy-Side Adoption

Generative AI Crosses From Pilot to Production at Nearly Half of Large Hedge Funds

A new survey from AIMA (the Alternative Investment Management Association, the main global trade body for hedge funds) finds that 47% of mid-to-large hedge funds now run generative AI tools in production — meaning the tools have cleared compliance, security, and model-risk review and are used in live workflows, not just sandboxes. Adoption is most concentrated among quantitative and multi-strategy funds managing more than $5 billion, where AI is used for research synthesis, code generation, and first-pass due diligence. The survey notes a sharp jump from the prior year's results, when most funds described their AI use as exploratory.

"In production" is a meaningfully higher bar than "we tried ChatGPT" — it means a fund's legal, compliance, and infosec teams signed off on data handling and model behavior. For anyone building AI tools for portfolio managers and analysts, 47% in production is the number that defines the addressable market today, and it tells you the buyers you're selling to already have a model-risk review process you need to design for, not educate from scratch.

Agentic AI

Agentic Payments

An AI Agent Just Bought Concert Tickets With Real Money — Europe's First End-to-End Agentic Payment

At Money20/20 Europe on June 2, Mastercard, ING, and Worldline demonstrated what they describe as Europe's first live, end-to-end agentic payment: an AI shopping assistant searched for concert tickets within a set budget, selected an option, and completed the purchase using a real payment credential — but only after the human shopper explicitly approved the specific transaction. The companies built the flow on emerging agentic-commerce standards that let an AI agent hold a constrained, revocable payment credential rather than a shopper's full card details.

The interesting design decision isn't that an agent can pay — it's where the human-approval checkpoint sits in the flow. Every agentic-finance product, from a trading-desk research assistant to a robo-advisor rebalancing tool, has to answer the same question: does the agent act and then report back, or does it pause and wait for sign-off? This is the first production example to show the latter, and it's a useful reference design.

Research Tools

JPMorgan's IndexGPT and Goldman's Multi-Model Assistant Show Two Different Bets on How to Productize the Analyst

JPMorgan has expanded internal access to its LLM Suite — including Coach AI and IndexGPT, tools built specifically for research and index analysis — to roughly 50,000 employees, all running on a single in-house model stack the bank controls end-to-end. Goldman Sachs has taken the opposite architectural bet: its assistant, now used by about 10,000 employees, lets staff draw on a choice of outside models including ChatGPT, Gemini, and Llama, depending on the task. Both banks report the tools are now used for first-draft research summaries, document review, and code generation.

These are two opposing answers to a question every enterprise AI buyer faces: build deep on one model you control, or stay model-agnostic and let users pick. JPMorgan's bet optimizes for security and consistency; Goldman's optimizes for capability and avoids lock-in. Before picking a side for your own analyst tools, ask which failure mode you're more exposed to — a model that underperforms, or a vendor that disappears.

Regulation

FCA · UK

The FCA's Mills Review Reports to Its Board This Summer — and Could Reshape How Far "Principles-Based" Stretches

The UK's Financial Conduct Authority launched the Mills Review on January 27, led by senior FCA executive Sheldon Mills, to examine how AI will reshape retail financial services by 2030. The regulator has been explicit that it does not plan to write a new AI-specific rulebook; instead, the review tests whether existing frameworks — Consumer Duty (the FCA's rule requiring firms to act in customers' best interests), the Senior Managers and Certification Regime, and operational resilience rules — can stretch to cover AI-driven advice and decision-making without new legislation. Findings go to the FCA board this summer.

A "no new rulebook" stance puts the burden on firms to interpret how 2020-era principles apply to AI-generated advice, a much harder compliance problem than checking boxes against a prescriptive new rule. If you're building AI tools that touch retail-facing advice or decisions in any FCA-regulated market, the review's summer findings will tell you whether "the model was transparent and explainable" is still considered sufficient, or whether the bar is about to move.

Model Suspension · Day 10

Anthropic Said the Fable 5 Ban Would Lift "in Coming Days" — Five Days Later, It's Also the Subscriber Refund Deadline

Speaking in Seoul on June 17, an Anthropic executive said the company was "very confident" the Fable 5 and Mythos 5 suspension would lift "in the coming days." Five days on, both models remain offline with no public end date. The timing has compounded: today, June 22, is also the last day Fable 5 remains free to use on Anthropic's Pro, Max, Team, and Enterprise plans before the company's previously announced usage-based billing — $10 per million input tokens, $50 per million output tokens — takes effect on June 23, regardless of whether access has actually been restored.

A confident public timeline that slips by five-plus days, layered on top of a billing deadline that doesn't move with it, is exactly the kind of vendor-communication failure that erodes enterprise trust fastest. If you depend on a single frontier model for a customer-facing product, this is the live case study for why your contract needs an SLA credit clause tied to suspension events, not just uptime — "confident" is not a commitment.

From the Labs

Anthropic · Infrastructure

Anthropic Locks In 3.5 More Gigawatts of Google TPU Capacity as Claude's Run-Rate Revenue Passes $30 Billion

Anthropic has expanded its compute partnership with Google and Broadcom, securing access to an additional 3.5 gigawatts of Google TPU (Tensor Processing Unit, Google's custom AI chip) capacity from 2027, on top of the roughly 1 gigawatt it already uses — part of a broader $50 billion commitment to US compute infrastructure. The expansion lands alongside word that Claude's run-rate revenue (annualized revenue based on the most recent month) has passed $30 billion, up from roughly $9 billion at the end of 2025, with more than 1,000 customers each spending over $1 million a year.

Anthropic is deliberately diversifying its compute supply across Google, Amazon, and now an expanded multi-chip footprint — the same lesson the Fable 5 export-control saga is teaching about single points of failure, just applied to hardware instead of regulation. The $30 billion run-rate figure is also the number every analyst modeling Anthropic's autumn IPO will anchor to, so it's worth tracking how that number evolves over the next two quarters.

OpenAI · Product Strategy

OpenAI Makes GPT-5.5 Instant the New ChatGPT Default — Choosing Speed Over a Bigger Flagship

OpenAI has rolled out GPT-5.5 Instant as the new default model for ChatGPT, a faster and cheaper variant tuned for everyday queries, while keeping the full GPT-5.5 model — released in April — available for users who select it for deeper reasoning or agentic coding tasks. The move mirrors Google's decision in May to make Gemini 3.5 Flash, not its larger Pro model, the default for most users.

Three major labs in a row — Google, OpenAI, and (with Claude's lower-cost tiers) Anthropic — have now chosen to default consumers to the cheap, fast model rather than the most capable one. That's a strong signal about where real-world usage volume actually sits, and a good argument for defaulting your own internal analyst tools to a fast, cheap tier with an explicit opt-in for the expensive reasoning model, rather than the reverse.

AI Adoption

94% of Product Managers Now Use AI Daily — and It's Buying Back Entire Workweeks

Productboard's 2026 survey of product managers finds 94% now use AI tools daily or often in their work, with roughly half describing AI as deeply embedded in their core workflow rather than a side tool. Respondents report saving an average of about four hours per task on activities like writing specs and synthesizing research, adding up to roughly 33 hours reclaimed per month across a typical PM's workload.

At 94% daily usage, AI fluency among product managers is now table stakes, not a differentiator — the real question shifts to what's done with the reclaimed time. Track whether your own team is using saved hours to ship more low-value tickets faster, or to spend more time on the discovery and prioritization work AI still can't do — that distinction is the actual measure of whether AI adoption is paying off.

Tooling Standards

MCP Adoption Hit 97 Million Monthly Downloads — and It's Becoming a Board-Level Question for SaaS Vendors

Model Context Protocol (MCP, the open standard that lets AI agents connect to outside tools and data sources), grew from roughly 100,000 to 97 million monthly SDK downloads between early 2025 and March 2026, with more than 10,000 active public MCP servers now listed. A 2026 Stacklok survey found 41% of software organizations already run MCP servers in limited or broad production use, and Gartner forecasts 40% of enterprise applications will ship with AI agent capabilities by year-end, up from under 5% a year ago.

As external AI agents become a real channel through which work gets done, internal tools that aren't reachable by MCP become invisible to that channel entirely. If you're scoping a roadmap for analyst-facing tools, exposing your platform's core capabilities as an MCP server is increasingly a more durable investment than building one more standalone AI chat feature on top of it.
