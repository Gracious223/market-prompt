Public Markets

Lead Story

SpaceX Pays $60 Billion for Cursor Four Days After Its Record IPO — the Largest Startup Acquisition in History

SpaceX announced on June 16 that it will acquire Anysphere — the company behind Cursor, the AI coding assistant used by more than 30 million software developers — for $60 billion in an all-stock deal (meaning SpaceX pays using its own newly public shares rather than cash). The acquisition price makes this the largest purchase of a venture-backed startup on record, surpassing Microsoft's $69 billion Activision deal on any startup-origin comparison. Cursor had reached roughly $2.6 billion in annualised revenue (the pace of sales projected over a full year). SpaceX's AI division, SpaceXAI, had already been jointly training a proprietary model with Cursor on its Colossus supercomputing cluster for months before the announcement; that model is expected to ship inside both Cursor and Grok Build in the near term.

SpaceX debuted on Nasdaq at $135 per share on June 12, closed its first day at $161 after a 19% gain, then used that market credibility to buy the most widely used AI coding tool on Earth within the same week. For investors and AI product leaders, the signal is unambiguous: AI coding tools are no longer SaaS subscriptions (software sold as a monthly service) — they are strategic infrastructure, the same category as cloud computing and chip manufacturing. When a newly public $2-trillion company's first act is this kind of acquisition, it redraws every valuation and competitive map in enterprise software.

Earnings

HPE Posts Record $10.7 Billion Quarter — CEO Issues 2027 Guidance in June Because "There Is Real Demand"

Hewlett Packard Enterprise reported fiscal second-quarter (the three months ending April 2026) revenue of $10.7 billion on June 1 — up 40% from a year ago and well ahead of the $9.8 billion analysts had forecast. Adjusted earnings per share (a company's profit divided by its number of shares, after stripping out one-time costs) came in at $0.79, beating HPE's own guidance range of $0.51–0.55 by 47%. The AI server business drove the entire result: server revenue hit $5.45 billion, up 32.7% year over year. CEO Antonio Neri was so confident in AI infrastructure demand that he issued forward guidance through 2027 — something companies rarely do mid-year — telling investors: "There is real demand. I don't see any AI demand peak on the horizon."

HPE's blowout quarter confirms that the Nvidia data-centre story is not a one-company trade — demand is propagating down the full AI infrastructure stack to servers, networking, and storage. For analysts building AI sector models, HPE's raised 2027 guidance suggests the infrastructure buildout cycle has years of runway left; for PMs, it signals that compute costs are unlikely to fall quickly, making efficiency a durable competitive advantage to design into any AI product from day one.

Venture Capital

Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and BlackRock Back Jeff Bezos's "Artificial General Engineer" at a $41 Billion Valuation

Prometheus — a physical-world AI startup co-founded by Jeff Bezos and Vik Bajaj (former co-founder of Verily, Google's life-sciences arm) — raised $12 billion at a $41 billion valuation on June 11. The investors are a who's-who of Wall Street: JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, and BlackRock, alongside Bezos himself. Prometheus is building what it calls an "artificial general engineer" — software that can autonomously design and manufacture complex physical systems, from jet engines to drug compounds, with minimal human input. This was the company's second fundraise; it launched late last year with an initial $6.2 billion raise. The 150-person team is split across San Francisco, London, and Zurich, and the bulk of the new capital will fund computing infrastructure.

When the three largest financial institutions in the US co-invest in a single AI startup, it is both a strategic bet and a market signal: Wall Street believes physical-world AI automation is the next trillion-dollar category after language models. For analysts tracking sector fundamentals, Prometheus's target industries — aerospace, defence, pharma, and advanced manufacturing — are all areas where AI-driven engineering automation could compress product cycles and restructure labour costs at scale, changing sector dynamics far more than any single company's earnings guidance.

Regulation

US Policy · Frontier AI

Trump's New AI Order Gives Washington a 30-Day Sneak Preview of the Most Powerful Models — But No Company Has to Comply

President Trump signed an executive order (a presidential directive that carries the force of law without requiring Congress's approval) titled "Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security" on June 2. Its core request: AI companies voluntarily give the US government access to their most powerful frontier models (the most capable AI systems available) up to 30 days before releasing them to the public. The government would use that window to test for dangerous capabilities, particularly in cybersecurity. The order explicitly states it creates no mandatory licensing or preclearance requirement — participation is a request, not a rule. Most implementation deadlines fall around July 2; a secure deployment framework is due in early August. The order was signed privately, weeks after Trump postponed a ceremony with major tech CEOs because he "didn't like certain aspects of it" — the most significant change from the draft was trimming the review window from 90 days to 30.

A voluntary framework today becomes a mandatory framework after a major incident. Three of the four largest frontier AI labs — OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — are almost certain to comply publicly to avoid appearing obstructionist ahead of their own IPOs or Congressional appearances. For investors pricing AI platform risk, the 30-day window is the thin end of a regulatory wedge: the precedent for government pre-release access now exists, and any future administration can tighten it significantly — adding compliance costs and launch delays that are not yet priced into AI valuations.

From the Labs

Anthropic Alignment · AI Safety

Anthropic Finds Human Attackers Can Slip Past AI Safety Monitors 1-in-4 Times — A Problem for Banks Building "AI to Watch AI"

Anthropic's Alignment Science team published SLEIGHT-Bench — a new benchmark (a standardised test used to measure and compare AI system performance) — that stress-tests AI monitoring systems: the AI models that watch other AI agents for dangerous or deceptive behaviour. The key finding: human attackers who study an AI monitor's weaknesses can bypass it roughly 25% of the time using carefully constructed strategies. In a METR red-team exercise (a security test where a team deliberately tries to break a system) against Anthropic's own production monitor, human-generated attack strategies bypassed it 2.5 out of 10 times; model-generated attacks managed only 1 out of 15. The benchmark dataset and evaluation framework are open-sourced so other researchers can stress-test their own monitoring systems.

Yesterday's issue covered the FSB recommending that banks use AI to monitor AI. Anthropic's new benchmark shows that AI monitors — even frontier ones — have exploitable blind spots that grow more dangerous as the AI being monitored becomes more capable. If you are building or procuring AI governance infrastructure for a financial institution, "we have an AI monitor" is not the end of the sentence — you need its failure rate under adversarial conditions, and a 25% bypass rate on human-authored attacks is a material risk, not a theoretical edge case.

AI Product Management

Product Strategy

SpaceX Spent $60 Billion on a Coding Tool — What That Tells Every AI PM Who Builds on Third-Party Infrastructure

The SpaceX-Cursor deal is not just an M&A story (when one company buys another). It is a product strategy signal. SpaceX's first major move after the largest IPO in history was to buy the most widely used AI coding assistant on Earth — not to add features, but to embed its own proprietary models into 30 million developers' daily workflows and own that data pipeline. This is the same playbook Microsoft ran when it integrated GPT-4 into GitHub Copilot in 2023; Copilot now generates over $10 billion in annual revenue. The difference is scale and speed: SpaceX executed the same move in a single week, using public-market stock as currency. Cursor's enterprise customers — the companies paying for seat licences (subscriptions per user) — will now route their code and workflow data through a SpaceX-controlled model.

If your team's workflow depends on a third-party AI coding tool, research assistant, or data pipeline, the SpaceX-Cursor deal is a direct warning. Neutral tools become acquisition targets once they reach sufficient scale, and after an acquisition, models, pricing, and data terms can all change. The practical implication for your PRD (the product requirements document that tells engineers what to build): wherever your product relies on an AI tool a large platform player could want to own, build in a switching plan — not because you need it today, but because the consolidation wave the SpaceX deal represents has clearly started.
