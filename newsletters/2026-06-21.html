Public Markets

IPO · Week 1

SpaceX Closed Its First Full Trading Week at $185 — but the 43-Point Analyst Spread Says Wall Street Still Can't Price It

SpaceX (Nasdaq: SPCX) completed the largest IPO (the first time a company sells its shares to the public) in history on June 12, raising $75 billion at $135 per share. The stock closed that day at $160.95, then surged to an all-time high of $225.64 on June 16 before pulling back to roughly $185 by week's end — still up 37% from the IPO price but down 18% from the peak. Day-two trading volume topped 244 million shares, exceeding the dollar turnover of the S&P 500 ETF. Analyst price targets now span from CFRA's sell rating at $115 — "requires a 20-to-25-year time horizon to justify" — to NewStreet Research's buy at $165, a 43-percentage-point gap that reflects a fundamental disagreement about how fast Starlink (the satellite internet business) and launch revenues can compound.

SpaceX is now the third-largest company ever to go public, and its first-week volatility is a real-time proxy for how institutional investors (the pension funds and asset managers that buy large blocks of shares) are pricing AI-adjacent infrastructure companies. The 43-point analyst spread — on a stock that has been public for nine days — tells you that Wall Street does not yet have a consensus model for valuing companies whose revenue depends on future AI demand curves. That same pricing uncertainty will face Anthropic and OpenAI when they list this autumn.

AI Infrastructure · Earnings

HPE's AI Server Business Grew 40% Last Quarter — and the Company Just Moved Its Five-Year Plan Two Years Closer

Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE) reported its fiscal Q2 2026 results on June 1, powered by record enterprise demand for AI infrastructure. Total revenue rose 40% year-on-year — partly because HPE completed the acquisition of Juniper Networks (a networking company) during the quarter, but the AI server segment outperformed independently of that deal. Cloud and AI revenue grew 22.9% to $7.7 billion, with operating profit margins doubling to 12.4% — from 6.6% the prior year. HPE also launched the ProLiant DL394 Gen12 server, built specifically for agentic AI workloads (tasks where an AI system chains multiple decisions together rather than answering a single question). The stock rose roughly 16% on the day results dropped; HPE raised its full-year revenue growth guidance to 29–33% and said it is now two years ahead of its fiscal 2028 long-term financial plan.

HPE's results confirm that AI spending is flowing all the way through the hardware supply chain — from Nvidia chip sales to server assemblers to enterprise procurement budgets. If HPE's AI server backlog (orders placed but not yet shipped) holds at record levels into Q3, it becomes one of the best leading indicators of how much AI infrastructure capex (long-term investment in physical equipment) will enter the market in 2027 — directly relevant to anyone sizing AI company valuations right now.

Regulation

AI Policy · Executive Order

The White House Set Up a Voluntary 30-Day AI Review — but the Channel That Shut Down Fable 5 Bypasses It Entirely

On June 2, President Trump signed an executive order (a direct presidential directive with the force of law) titled "Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security." The order does three things: it creates an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse — a shared database where companies and the government can flag and fix software vulnerabilities — it establishes a voluntary scheme in which AI developers give the government up to 30 days of early access to frontier (most capable) models before public release, and it directs federal prosecutors toward criminal cases involving AI-enabled attacks. The order explicitly prohibits any mandatory government licensing or pre-clearance requirement for AI models. The Fable 5 and Mythos 5 suspension nine days later, however, arrived through a separate legal instrument: export control law (rules governing what technology can be shared with foreign parties), administered by the Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security, which operates independently of this executive order.

The voluntary review framework and export control enforcement run in parallel with no coordination mechanism between them. For enterprise procurement teams and AI product builders, this means that even a model that passes a voluntary 30-day pre-release review could still be suspended via a separate national security channel with zero warning — precisely what happened to Fable 5. Understanding which regulatory authority can shut down which AI product is now a basic vendor risk assessment question.

Watch Closely

Model Suspension · IPO Risk

Day Nine of the Fable 5 Deadlock: A Commerce Secretary Letter, a South Korean Firm With China Ties, and a White House Demand No One Thinks Is Realistic

Nine days after the US government suspended Fable 5 and Mythos 5, the deadlock appears to be deepening. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick sent a direct letter warning Anthropic against distributing either model to "foreign persons" — a broader legal category than the original directive, which targeted foreign nationals. The Washington Post reported that Anthropic's approved access list had "ballooned" in the weeks before the shutdown, including a South Korean firm with suspected ties to Chinese state-backed investors — the detail that appears to have accelerated the government's action. The New York Times obtained internal employee messages in which staff describe the lab as "unfairly targeted" and note that the White House's demand to block all jailbreaks (techniques used to bypass an AI's built-in limits) is "unrealistic to implement." The Rundown AI, citing sources close to the situation, reported both sides remain at an impasse with no timeline for restoration.

Anthropic's S-1 (the official document filed with the US Securities and Exchange Commission — the main financial regulator — before selling shares to the public for the first time) will be publicly available within weeks of the planned autumn listing. Every additional day offline is a sentence in that document's risk factors section. A Commerce Secretary warning letter is a harder fact to explain to institutional investors than an eight-day technical outage — it shifts the narrative from "compliance hiccup" to "unresolved regulatory dispute," which changes how investors model the probability of a future recurrence.

From the Labs

Anthropic Alignment · AI Safety

No New Alignment Posts — but the Fable 5 Deadlock Is the Clearest Live Test the Field Has Ever Produced

The Anthropic Alignment Science blog (alignment.anthropic.com) has published no new posts since May 2026. The most recent research, SLEIGHT-Bench, measured how often human attackers can bypass AI safety monitors (automated systems that watch an AI's outputs for dangerous content), finding a roughly one-in-four success rate in adversarial tests — meaning skilled attackers can sneak past AI watchdogs about 25% of the time. That benchmark now has a live policy consequence: the government's shutdown order for Fable 5 was triggered by exactly the kind of narrow adversarial jailbreak SLEIGHT-Bench was designed to detect. Separately, the White House executive order signed June 2 explicitly named AI monitor evasion as a federal criminal concern — one of the first times a government document has borrowed a concept directly from alignment research to define a legal category. There are no new posts to report today; we check this page daily and will cover any new research immediately.

Alignment researchers and government regulators are now responding to the same empirical event — the Fable 5 jailbreak — with different vocabularies and different enforcement tools: one calling it "narrow," the other calling it a national security risk. For banks, healthcare providers, and defence contractors writing AI procurement policies, the Fable 5 situation is the first empirical data point on what happens when a jailbreak crosses the government's shutdown threshold — and there is still no agreed framework for deciding where that line sits.

AI Product Management

Product Strategy · Open Weights

Four Open-Weight Models Filled the Gap Left by Fable 5 Within 24 Hours — and the Switching Cost Was Zero

While Fable 5 and Mythos 5 remain suspended, three open-weight (downloadable and self-hostable — meaning you run them on your own servers, not a cloud API) alternatives shipped in the same window. Cohere released North Mini Code on June 9, three days before the shutdown. Moonshot shipped Kimi K2.7-Code (a large language model focused on software coding) the same afternoon as the government directive. Zhipu opened GLM 5.2 the following day — at 5:21 PM, the same minute as the shutdown order the day before — with downloadable model weights (the underlying files that define the model) promised within a week. According to The New Stack, all four models offer capabilities comparable to the specific functions that triggered the US security concern, meaning teams that needed a replacement had downloadable options with similar performance within 24 hours of the ban, at zero marginal cost beyond their own compute (server processing time).

The Fable 5 ban has produced the clearest evidence to date that AI switching costs are lower than most enterprise procurement teams assumed. A product team running on Fable 5 on June 11 could have had a comparable open-weight model self-hosted by June 13 — which changes every conversation about vendor lock-in, SLA risk (a service-level agreement is a written commitment about uptime made to enterprise customers), and make-vs-buy decisions for anyone evaluating AI providers right now.

AI Infrastructure · Chips

Qualcomm Is Reportedly Bidding $8–10 Billion for Tenstorrent — the Opening Move of AI Chip Consolidation

Qualcomm (Nasdaq: QCOM) is in early talks to acquire Tenstorrent, an AI chip startup founded by chip architect Jim Keller, for between $8 billion and $10 billion, The Information first reported on June 16. That price is roughly four times Tenstorrent's December 2024 Series D valuation (a late-stage private funding round) of $2.6 billion. Tenstorrent builds AI accelerator chips — processors designed specifically for AI workloads — using RISC-V, an open-source chip architecture that does not require licensing fees from Arm or Intel. Its chips target data centre inference: the step where a deployed AI model generates its answer for a real user, which is increasingly the largest ongoing cost of running AI at scale. Qualcomm's stock rose about 4% on the day the report broke. If completed, the deal would be the largest AI chip acquisition since AMD bought Xilinx for $49 billion in 2022.

Nvidia currently earns roughly 80% of global AI training chip revenue, but inference — the ongoing cost of running deployed models at scale — is where the next decade of AI spending goes, and that market is structurally more competitive. For AI product managers setting multi-year infrastructure strategy, the Qualcomm-Tenstorrent talks signal that the inference chip market is entering a consolidation phase — which will affect cost curves, hardware availability, and vendor relationships for any team planning AI infrastructure over the next three years.
