Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

The vocabulary of financial markets is the vocabulary of your users' daily decisions. Here are six terms that come up constantly in fixed income, equity, and risk conversations — and that will sharpen how you frame AI product opportunities.

Liquidity
How quickly an asset can be bought or sold without meaningfully moving its price. Highly liquid assets (e.g. large-cap equities, US Treasuries) trade easily; illiquid assets (e.g. private credit) can be hard to exit. AI tools that improve price discovery matter most in illiquid markets.
Short Selling
Borrowing shares and selling them at today's price, expecting to buy them back cheaper later. If correct, the trader profits from the decline. AI that surfaces bearish signals or detects overvaluation is directly relevant to short-selling strategies on the buy side.
Market Capitalisation
Total value of a company's outstanding shares (share price × total shares). Used to classify companies: large-cap (above ~$10B), mid-cap, small-cap. AI screening tools typically allow portfolio managers to filter by market cap as a primary dimension.
Beta
Measures how much a stock moves relative to the broader market. A beta of 1.2 means the stock moves 20% more than the market in either direction. High-beta stocks amplify gains and losses. AI risk systems model portfolio beta exposure in real time to flag when concentration risk is building.
Basis Points (bps)
One basis point = 0.01%. The universal unit for expressing small interest rate or yield changes in fixed income. "A 25 bps cut" means rates fell by 0.25%. When analysts discuss model performance fees or yield spreads, they speak in basis points — know this before any stakeholder meeting.
Credit Spread
The difference in yield between a corporate bond and a comparable government bond. A widening spread signals rising perceived risk — investors demand more return to hold corporate debt. AI models monitoring credit spread movements can flag deteriorating credit conditions before rating agencies act.

FCA Publishes Mills Review: Four Systemic AI Shifts, Seven Recommendations

Published 6 July 2026, the Mills Review is the FCA's most comprehensive AI analysis to date. It identifies four systemic shifts: transformation of firm operations, evolution of consumer journeys, reshaping of market competition, and amplification of fraud and cyber risks. Key finding: only 20% of consumers would trust AI acting autonomously on their behalf. The Senior Managers Regime still applies — firms remain accountable for AI-driven outcomes regardless of which model is running.

Why It Matters

The FCA isn't writing new AI rules — it's clarifying that existing accountability frameworks already cover AI decisions. For your product, that means explainability and auditability are regulatory obligations from day one, not a later compliance checklist.

Research & Think About
  • Read the Mills Review — fca.org.uk — particularly the section on accountability in AI-assisted decisions
  • Under the Senior Managers & Certification Regime: who at your firm is accountable if an AI tool causes harm?
  • How would you build explainability into a product feature from the PRD stage — not as an afterthought?

MCP Is Standardising How AI Connects to Tools — PMs Need to Understand Why

Model Context Protocol (MCP) has emerged as the standard for connecting AI models to external tools, databases, and APIs. Combined with "vibe coding" — PMs using AI to build their own prototypes without engineers — the technical baseline required to be an effective AI PM in 2026 has risen materially. PMs who can't engage with these concepts are losing influence in product design conversations.

Why It Matters

MCP determines what data your AI product can access and how. Not understanding it means ceding a critical product decision to engineering — and then inheriting the consequences of a choice you didn't make.

Research & Think About
