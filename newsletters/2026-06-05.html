AI in Public Markets

Equities

Micron Tops $1 Trillion as the Memory Trade Becomes the AI Trade

The market is surging — and it's still all about AI, but the leadership has rotated. Micron crossed $1 trillion in market value this week, now worth more than JPMorgan Chase, with shares up nearly 1,000% over the past year. SanDisk is up more than 4,500% over the same window. The bottleneck has moved from raw compute to the high-bandwidth memory that feeds it, and capital is repricing the picks-and-shovels layer accordingly.

Why it matters for you: The "AI winner" is no longer a single name — it's a supply chain that re-ranks as bottlenecks shift (compute → memory → next: power, networking). A tool that tracks where the constraint is this quarter, not just who sells chips, is far more useful to a PM than another flat list of AI tickers.

IPO Watch

The Trillion-Dollar IPO Window Gets a Date: SpaceX on 12 June, OpenAI Next

SpaceX is expected to go public on 12 June, with OpenAI lined up to follow, days after Anthropic confidentially filed its own paperwork. All three are expected to debut valued between $1T and $2T — figures that would place them among the world's dozen largest companies by market cap on day one. The Motley Fool notes the harder question is timing: index inclusion (e.g. the S&P 500) lags the listing and depends on float, profitability, and seasoning rules.

Why it matters for you: "When does it enter the index" is a different — and more tractable — question than "is it a good company." Passive flows hinge on inclusion mechanics, not narrative. That's a concrete, rules-based signal an internal tool can compute and flag; the kind of unglamorous edge analysts actually pay for.

Buy-Side Stack

Perplexity vs. Bloomberg: the Fight for the Analyst's Desktop Goes Agentic

Two visions of the research workstation are colliding. Bloomberg's ASKB — billed as the biggest rethink of the terminal in its history — runs a coordinated network of agents that build screens and full research reports on the fly, blending Bloomberg data, 800+ sell-side providers, and alt-data (card transactions, foot traffic, satellite imagery) with a firm's own house views, using models from Bloomberg and Anthropic. Against it, Perplexity Computer offers a ~$200/mo challenger wired to 40+ live data tools including SEC EDGAR, FactSet, and S&P Global.

Why it matters for you: This is the build-vs-buy decision playing out at full scale. Bloomberg's moat is proprietary + alt-data and integration; the challenger's wedge is price and open tool-access. When you scope your internal tool, be honest about which side you're on — you almost certainly can't out-data Bloomberg, so your edge has to be workflow depth and your users' proprietary context.

From the Labs

Frontier Models

Microsoft and Google Crash the AI-Coding Party — Anthropic's Stronghold

Anthropic has zoomed ahead of the field in generative AI largely on the back of Claude Code, its coding assistant — and just shipped Claude Opus 4.8. Now Microsoft and Google are making a concerted push into AI coding models, leaning on their balance sheets and cloud distribution to lure developers away. Coding is the clearest product-market fit the labs have found; everyone now wants the seat.

Why it matters for you: Note where the competition is fiercest — coding, because the value is measurable (PRs merged, bugs caught) and the workflow is well-defined. That's the template for a defensible AI product: pick a domain where output quality is verifiable and tied to a real workflow, not an open-ended "assistant." Distribution (cloud, IDE) is decisive once quality converges.

AI Product Management

Unit Economics

GitHub Copilot Moves to Usage-Based Pricing — the Reckoning Reaches the Bestseller

GitHub Copilot is shifting to usage-based pricing in June 2026. The reason is brutally simple: flat-fee seats stopped working once agentic features ran up real inference bills — some heavy users reportedly cost Microsoft ~$80/month while paying $10. Even the category's flagship product can't subsidise unbounded token consumption indefinitely.

Why it matters for you: Pricing is a product decision, and "unlimited for a flat fee" is a trap when your marginal cost is live inference. If you're building an internal AI tool, model cost-per-action from day one and design the UX so usage is legible to the user — otherwise your power users quietly become your biggest loss centre, exactly as they did for Copilot.

Watch Closely

Systemic Risk

The Circular Deals Underwriting the Boom Draw Sharper Scrutiny

Bloomberg's tracker of "AI circular deals" lays out how Microsoft, OpenAI and Nvidia keep paying each other — Nvidia invests in OpenAI, which buys Nvidia GPUs to fill data centres, and round it goes. Analysts warn this creates two systemic risks: reflexive demand (one firm slowing capex drags revenue across the whole cluster) and mispriced capacity (build decisions rely on internal signals rather than independent market validation). Projected data-centre spend: ~$3T in the coming years.

Why it matters for you: Revenue that recirculates inside a closed loop isn't the same as revenue from independent end-demand — and that distinction is exactly what an S-1 or an analyst should stress-test. For your own products, the lesson is the same: validate demand outside the hype loop. Pilots driven by FOMO budgets are the first thing cut when the reflexive cycle turns.
