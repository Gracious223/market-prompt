Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

New week, new vocabulary rotation. These six terms come up constantly when analysts discuss execution, valuation, and macro positioning — exactly the territory where AI tools are being aimed first.

Order Book
The real-time list of buy and sell orders for a security, organised by price level. AI execution agents read order-book depth to decide how and when to place trades — a core concept if your product touches trading.
Float
The number of a company's shares actually available for public trading (excluding insider and locked-up stock). Low-float stocks are more volatile — and a frequent trap for AI screening tools that don't account for it.
Credit Spread
The yield difference between a corporate bond and a "risk-free" government bond of similar maturity — the market's price for default risk. Widening spreads are an early-warning signal AI risk models are built to catch.
Mark-to-Market
Valuing a position at its current market price rather than its purchase price. Daily mark-to-market is what makes drawdowns visible in real time — and why "explainability" in an AI valuation tool isn't optional.
Quantitative Easing (QE)
A central bank buying assets (usually government bonds) to inject liquidity and push down long-term rates. Understanding QE cycles helps you grasp why "the macro backdrop" dominates so much portfolio-manager conversation.
Correlation
A statistical measure of how two assets move relative to each other, from -1 to +1. "Diversification" only works when correlations are low — which is precisely Goldman's concern about today's AI-driven rally (see above).

Citadel Will Start Paying Rival Hedge Funds for Their Trading Ideas

Citadel's Global Quantitative Strategies arm is building a program to buy investment signals directly from other funds' discretionary managers — feeding human judgment into systematic models, an evolution of the 20-year-old "alpha capture" playbook.

Platform Shift

Apple Rebuilds Siri on Gemini — Then Lets You Replace It Entirely

Apple's overhauled Siri runs on a custom Gemini model under a roughly $1B/year licensing deal. But the bigger move is "Extensions" — letting users swap in ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude as their default assistant, breaking from Apple's closed-ecosystem instinct.

Why It Matters

Apple just validated model-agnostic design at consumer scale — the same principle behind Goldman's internal AI Assistant. Vendor lock-in is becoming a strategic liability everywhere.

Research & Think About
  • Map what it would take to make your product swap models without users noticing — what breaks first?
  • Ask your engineers what "model routing" means in production systems, and why it's getting more common
  • Consider: does giving users model choice increase or reduce your product's accountability for outputs?
