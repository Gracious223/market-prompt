Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

These terms appear daily in analyst conversations, earnings calls, and risk reports. Fluency means you can follow the room — and ask better product questions. Six fresh additions to your working vocabulary, each with a direct read-across to the AI tools your users need.

Yield Curve
A chart plotting bond yields across different maturities (e.g. 2-year vs 10-year). A normal curve slopes upward; an inverted curve (short rates above long rates) is a recession signal. Macro analysts and fixed income PMs watch it constantly — AI tools that model curve shifts in real time are high-value.
P/E Ratio
Price-to-Earnings: a stock’s price divided by its earnings per share. A high P/E implies high growth expectations; a low P/E may signal undervaluation or distress. Analysts use it to compare companies within a sector. AI tools that surface P/E outliers across large universes accelerate screening significantly.
Free Cash Flow (FCF)
Cash a company generates after capital expenditure — the clearest measure of financial health independent of accounting choices. “FCF yield” (FCF divided by market cap) is a key valuation metric. Analysts building investment theses anchor them in FCF, making it essential vocabulary for your product conversations.
Beta
A measure of a stock’s volatility relative to the broader market. Beta of 1 = moves with the market; above 1 = more volatile; below 1 = more stable. Portfolio managers use beta to manage overall portfolio risk. AI tools that help optimise portfolio beta in real time are among the most sought-after quant applications.
Net Asset Value (NAV)
The value of a fund’s total assets minus liabilities, divided by shares outstanding. NAV is how fund investors measure what their investment is actually worth. For your users, NAV calculation and reporting is often a time-consuming manual process — a strong AI automation opportunity.
Drawdown
The peak-to-trough decline in a portfolio’s value over a given period. Maximum drawdown is a key risk metric — it tells you the worst loss an investor would have experienced. PMs managing drawdown risk are natural users of AI tools that monitor positions and flag deterioration before it compounds.

US Treasury Issues AI Risk Framework for Finance — 230 Control Objectives Now the Baseline

The Treasury’s Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework (FS AI RMF), released February 2026, maps 230 control objectives across the full AI lifecycle — adapted from NIST specifically for financial services. It is voluntary guidance today. Grant Thornton, Lowenstein Sandler, and others are warning it will shape auditor standards and examination benchmarks before most firms have finished reading it.

Why It Matters

Voluntary today means expected-by-auditors tomorrow. The 230 control objectives cover procurement, monitoring, and incident response across the full AI lifecycle. If your product roadmap doesn’t map to this framework, you are building compliance technical debt into your architecture from day one.
