Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

Today's edition covers the mechanics of trading and risk measurement your analysts use when evaluating whether a trade is feasible, fairly priced, and worth the risk. These terms come up when discussions shift from "what to buy" to "how to buy it — and whether we can afford to be wrong."

Short Selling
Borrowing a stock, selling it, then buying it back cheaper later to pocket the difference — a bet the price will fall. Risk: unlimited (a stock can rise indefinitely). AI tools are increasingly used to identify short opportunities from alternative data signals before consensus catches up.
Market Capitalisation
Share price × shares outstanding. Large-cap stocks (£10B+) are liquid and widely covered by analysts. Small-cap stocks are less liquid and under-researched — the area where AI research tools can surface the most edge over traditional analyst coverage.
Liquidity
How easily an asset can be bought or sold without materially moving its price. High-liquidity assets (large-cap equities, gilts) are easy to trade at scale. Low-liquidity assets (small-caps, private credit) are harder — your product must surface this constraint, not ignore it.
Sharpe Ratio
Return above the risk-free rate, divided by volatility — the standard measure of risk-adjusted performance. JPMorgan's AI agents beat the 60/40 portfolio on a Sharpe basis: not just higher returns, but better returns per unit of risk taken.
Benchmark
The index a portfolio is measured against — e.g. the S&P 500, FTSE 100, or a custom blended index. "Beating the benchmark" means generating alpha. AI tools that help analysts identify benchmark-outperforming positions are among the highest-value applications in the space.
Basis Points (bps)
One hundredth of a percentage point: 1 bps = 0.01%. Bond markets, fees, and performance discussions all run in basis points. When JPMorgan's AI beat the 60/40 by 0.7 percentage points, that's 70 bps — material at institutional scale.

FCA Speech: No Parallel AI Rulebook — Consumer Duty Is the Law for the Age of Agents

The FCA's companion speech to the Mills Review confirmed its regulatory position for the next cycle: no bespoke AI rules, principles-based oversight, and Consumer Duty as the primary anchor for AI-generated financial outputs. With over 80% of UK financial firms now actively deploying AI, the FCA's message is clear — get existing framework compliance right, starting now.

Why It Matters

This is your PRD's regulatory context in plain language. Every feature that generates a recommendation, output, or advice-adjacent insight must be defensible under Consumer Duty. No AI exemption is coming — design explainability in now, not at audit.

Research & Think About
  • Read the FCA speech in full — it's public at fca.org.uk and sets out precisely what the regulator expects from AI product teams
  • Identify which features in your current product roadmap touch Consumer Duty obligations — flag them before engineering scoping
  • Ask your compliance team: what does SM&CR personal accountability look like when an AI agent executes a multi-step financial workflow?
