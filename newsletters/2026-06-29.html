Public Markets

Index Change

Alphabet Joins the Dow Today — Now Every AI Giant Is a Blue-Chip Stock

Alphabet — the parent company of Google — officially replaced Verizon in the Dow Jones Industrial Average (a 30-company index of America's largest, most established businesses) at the start of today's trading, June 29. It joins Nvidia, Amazon, Apple, and Microsoft, meaning every company widely understood to be leading the AI race is now part of the oldest and most-watched stock index in the world. S&P Dow Jones Indices, which manages the Dow, said the change was made to better reflect artificial intelligence, cloud computing, and digital advertising — the forces now driving the economy — and away from traditional telecommunications.

Why this matters to you: When the Dow changes, index funds (investment funds that automatically hold every stock in the index in proportion) and pension funds that track it must buy Alphabet's shares and sell Verizon's — automatically routing new money into Alphabet regardless of individual investors' opinions. It's also a symbolic verdict: the keepers of Wall Street's oldest benchmark have formally declared that AI companies are now the bedrock of the American economy.

IPO Watch

SK Hynix Plans a $29 Billion Nasdaq Listing — the Memory Chip Powering AI Is Coming to America

SK Hynix — the South Korean company that manufactures the specialized high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips (a type of ultra-fast memory that Nvidia packs into every AI server, because AI models need to move massive amounts of data extremely quickly) — is planning to list its shares on the Nasdaq (America's main technology stock exchange) via ADRs (American depositary receipts, a legal structure that lets foreign companies sell shares directly to U.S. investors), targeting July 10 at a value of around $29 billion. That would eclipse both Alibaba's 2014 U.S. listing and Saudi Aramco's $25.6 billion offering, making it potentially the second-largest stock offering in history. HSBC analysts have published a price target 20% above the listing price, arguing that Hynix's HBM chips are the single most constrained component in the global AI buildout.

Why this matters to you: If you want a public-market way to invest in AI infrastructure rather than the AI companies themselves, SK Hynix's U.S. listing gives investors direct exposure to the physical memory layer that makes large AI models possible — the hardware no one can build around. Watch the July 10 debut closely; the pricing and first-day trading will signal how much appetite remains for AI supply-chain bets after months of market turbulence.

Tools for Analysts & PMs

AI Governance

Global Securities Regulators Just Published a Practical AI Oversight Toolkit — Here Is What Your Firm Needs to Know

IOSCO (the International Organisation of Securities Commissions — the global body that coordinates rules for stock and bond markets across more than 130 countries) published a final Supervisory Toolkit for AI Use in Capital Markets on May 25, 2026, and its public comment period closed June 26. The toolkit gives both regulators and the firms they oversee a structured framework for managing AI systems across four areas: governance and risk management, third-party and outsourcing risk, disclosure to investors, and recordkeeping. Critically, it explicitly covers agentic AI (systems that take autonomous actions — like executing trades or filing documents — without a human approving each step), not just traditional machine-learning tools.

Why this matters to you: IOSCO toolkits are not binding law, but they are the clearest advance signal of what securities regulators in 130+ countries will look for when they examine your firm's AI systems. If you are building or deploying AI inside a broker, asset manager, exchange, or clearinghouse anywhere in the world, this document is the de facto compliance preview — treat it as a checklist, not optional reading.

From the Labs

AI Hardware

OpenAI Built Its First Custom Chip in Nine Months — and Claims It Cuts AI Costs by 50% Against Nvidia GPUs

On June 24, OpenAI and Broadcom (a major semiconductor — computer chip — company) unveiled Jalapeño, OpenAI's first custom AI inference chip (a processor designed specifically to run AI models in real time, rather than to train them). Built in just nine months using Broadcom's manufacturing expertise and OpenAI's own AI models to accelerate parts of the design process, Jalapeño targets roughly 50% lower cost per AI query compared with current Nvidia GPU-based systems, according to Broadcom's CEO Hock Tan. The chip is set to begin deployment in prototype systems by end of 2026 and ramp to broader production through 2027 and 2028. The cost figures are self-reported and based on OpenAI's own testing, without independently verified comparison baselines.

Why this matters to you: Today, every ChatGPT conversation runs on Nvidia hardware. If Jalapeño hits its cost targets, OpenAI cuts its single largest operating expense and removes Nvidia as a bottleneck to profitability ahead of its expected IPO (first-time public stock offering). For Nvidia investors, this is the first concrete evidence that the company's biggest customers are actively engineering a path off its chips — and that the AI chip market is about to get genuinely competitive.

Market Dynamics

Gartner: 40% of Enterprise Apps Will Have Built-In AI Agents by Year End — Spending Jumps 139% in One Year

Gartner, the technology research firm, forecasts that 40% of enterprise applications (software used inside businesses for operations, sales, finance, and HR) will include built-in AI agents (software that takes autonomous actions on your behalf — scheduling meetings, filing reports, routing tickets, or summarizing research — without needing human sign-off at each step) by the end of 2026, up from under 5% in 2025. AI agent software spending is projected to reach $206.5 billion in 2026 — a 139% jump from $86.4 billion in 2025. AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, IBM, and Databricks have all publicly framed agentic AI as the next major layer of software, effectively positioning it as a new category between traditional applications and human workers.

Why this matters to you: If your product roadmap does not account for your end users gaining AI agent capabilities inside the tools they already use — from Salesforce to Excel to Bloomberg Terminal — you risk building a product that solves a problem users will soon delegate to an agent. The window to design AI-native workflows, rather than retrofitting them, is closing faster than most roadmaps currently assume.

Watch Closely

AI & Geopolitics

Anthropic Accuses Alibaba of Running the Largest Known AI Capability Theft in History

On June 24, Anthropic sent a formal letter to U.S. Senators Elizabeth Warren and Tim Scott alleging that Alibaba Group's Qwen AI lab ran what it called "the largest known distillation attack" on its Claude AI model. Distillation is a technique where a company trains a weaker AI model by feeding it millions of outputs from a more powerful one — effectively copying the smarter model's capabilities at a fraction of the cost. Anthropic alleges that Alibaba did this through 25,000 fraudulent accounts that ran 28.8 million conversations with Claude between April 22 and June 5, 2026, specifically targeting Claude's software-engineering and autonomous-reasoning abilities. Alibaba has denied the allegations. U.S. Senators Bill Hagerty and Andy Kim are already moving to add sanctions language to defense legislation targeting entities that conduct such campaigns.

Why this matters to you: This case will set the legal precedent for whether AI capabilities are intellectual property that can be protected — or a public good anyone can extract through clever account creation. The outcome shapes competitive moats for every AI company preparing to go public, and the legislative response could restrict which AI vendors regulated U.S. financial firms are permitted to use.

Regulation

Colorado Rewrote Its Landmark AI Law — the New Version Takes Effect January 1, 2027

Colorado — the first U.S. state to pass a comprehensive AI regulation — has now effectively restarted from scratch. On May 14, 2026, Governor Jared Polis signed SB 189, which repeals the original 2024 law's risk-based requirements and replaces them with a narrower framework focused on transparency and disclosure (telling users when they are interacting with AI and what data is being used) rather than substantive risk management obligations like mandatory impact assessments, anti-discrimination duties, and attorney-general reporting. The effective date was also pushed back from June 30, 2026, to January 1, 2027, with the state attorney general required to write implementing regulations before the law kicks in.

Why this matters to you: The reprieve is real, but the deadline is now fixed. For AI product managers building systems used in Colorado — particularly in high-stakes decisions (hiring, lending, insurance, healthcare) — January 1 is a hard compliance date. More importantly, Colorado's pivot away from the EU-style risk framework signals that U.S. state AI law is converging around disclosure requirements rather than pre-deployment safety obligations — which is a meaningfully different compliance burden to design for.
