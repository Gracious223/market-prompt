AI in Public Markets

IPO Watch

The Trillion-Dollar IPO Test: SpaceX Files, OpenAI Lines Up Behind It

SpaceX's S-1 went public on 20 May, with a roadshow expected the week of 4 June and a first trade targeted for mid-month. OpenAI is reportedly preparing to file confidentially within days. After years of mega-rounds in private markets, the two most-watched names of the AI era are about to be priced by public investors — the first real test of whether public-market discipline agrees with private valuations.

Why it matters for you: Your analysts and PMs will be modelling these names within weeks. The interesting product question: what does a defensible AI valuation actually rest on — revenue durability, compute contracts, or narrative? That framing is exactly the kind of judgement your tools should help surface, not bury.

Capex

Alphabet's $175–185B Capex Plan Triggers a Sell-Off

Alphabet guided to $175–185B of capital expenditure in 2026 — roughly double 2025's level — and investors responded by selling, questioning whether record AI spend will convert into earnings. It's a sharper version of the question now hanging over every hyperscaler: the spend is certain, the payback isn't.

Why it matters for you: The market is starting to price AI on unit economics, not vision decks. When you scope an AI product, the same discipline applies internally — cost-per-query and demonstrable ROI are becoming the questions that get budget approved or killed.

Buy-Side Stack

The Hedge-Fund AI Stack Splits Into Three Layers

A clear pattern emerged in this year's buy-side tooling round-ups: the funds getting real value aren't asking one platform to do everything. The stack now separates into a research layer (AlphaSense, Third Bridge — find and synthesise faster), an infrastructure layer (Palantir AIP, Blueflame AI — make fragmented proprietary and alternative data actionable, with the governance compliance demands), and an operational layer (the knowledge work around meetings, CRM, and internal comms).

Why it matters for you: This is the most useful build-vs-buy map you'll see. Pick which layer you're competing in before writing a line of PRD — trying to span all three is how internal AI tools die. Note that the infrastructure layer is where governance and auditability live; that's the hardest moat and likely your real differentiator for analysts.

From the Labs

Anthropic

Anthropic Ships Claude Opus 4.8 — and Passes OpenAI in Value

Anthropic shipped Claude Opus 4.8, claiming it tops OpenAI's GPT-5.5 and Google's Gemini 3.1 Pro on agentic coding, financial analysis, and computer use. Days later, on 28 May, it announced a $65B round at a ~$965B valuation — led by Altimeter, Dragoneer, Greenoaks, and Sequoia — nearly tripling February's $380B mark and vaulting past OpenAI as the most valuable AI startup in the world.

Why it matters for you: "Financial analysis" as a named benchmark category is the signal here — the frontier labs are now optimising for exactly your users' domain. Worth running Opus 4.8 against your own analyst workflows before committing to a model in any PRD; the default leader changed this month. (For the record: this newsletter was generated by Opus 4.8.)

Google I/O

Google's Gemini Spark Pushes From Assistants to Agents

At I/O, Google paired Gemini 3.5 Flash with Gemini Spark — a general-purpose agent that reasons across your connected apps rather than just answering prompts. The framing from Mountain View: the assistant era is giving way to autonomous agents that take multi-step action. The strategic tell remains speed and cost over a frontier behemoth.

Why it matters for you: "Agent that acts across connected systems" is the design pattern your analysts will soon expect by default. The hard part isn't the model — it's permissioning, auditability, and what happens when an agent acts on stale or wrong data. Scope those constraints early.

AI Product Management

Career & Role

CPO Report: The "Product Builder" Is Up 10x as Generalist PMs Decline

A 2026 CPO Insights report makes a blunt prediction: traditional product managers could "disappear" by 2030. Its data shows the number of Product Builders — a hybrid PM-engineering role — up 10x, while traditional PM headcount is down 30% across industries and as much as 70% in SaaS. The roles surviving are the ones closest to either building or to irreplaceable product judgement.

Why it matters for you: This continues last issue's "the role is splitting" thread — and it's a direct challenge to you. The defensible position isn't "AI PM" as a title; it's being able to prototype and reason about the system yourself. Push on your own gap here: can you stand up a working agent demo, or only spec one?

Bubble Risk

Circular AI Financing Draws Dot-Com Comparisons

The web of cross-investments and capacity commitments between Microsoft, OpenAI, Nvidia, Google, and Anthropic is drawing scrutiny for resembling the vendor-financing structures of the late dot-com era — money flowing in a circle that can inflate valuations. Morgan Stanley pegs 2025–28 global data-center spend near $3T, roughly half funded by private credit. Reports that OpenAI missed user and revenue targets added fresh fuel to crash talk.

Why it matters for you: "Is this revenue real or circular?" is a question your analysts will ask of every AI name — and increasingly, of internal AI projects too. Build for the downside case: if the funding climate tightens, the AI tools that survive are the ones with proven, measurable value, not promise.
