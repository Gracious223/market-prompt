Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

Today's edition focuses on the core valuation and risk concepts your analysts use daily to evaluate securities and portfolios. When your users talk about a stock being "expensive on a P/E basis" or a bond having "high duration risk," these are the terms they mean.

Yield Curve
A chart showing bond yields at different maturities. A normal curve slopes upward (longer = higher yield). An inverted curve — where short-term rates exceed long-term ones — is a classic recession signal your macro analysts watch closely.
Beta
A measure of how much a stock moves relative to the overall market. Beta of 1 = moves with the market. Beta of 2 = twice as volatile. AI tools that help size positions use beta to calibrate risk at the portfolio level.
P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
The price of a share divided by earnings per share. A P/E of 25 means investors pay £25 for every £1 of annual profit. High P/E signals growth expectations; low P/E may indicate undervaluation or distress.
Drawdown
The peak-to-trough decline in a portfolio's value over a given period. A 20% drawdown means the portfolio fell 20% from its high. Risk-averse mandates set maximum drawdown limits — AI tools must respect them.
ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)
A fund that tracks an index or basket of assets and trades on an exchange like a stock. ETFs are the dominant vehicle for passive investing and increasingly used to execute thematic AI investment views quickly.
Dividend Yield
Annual dividends paid by a company divided by its share price — expressed as a percentage. A 4% yield means £4 in annual income per £100 invested. Income-focused mandates (pension funds, insurers) screen heavily on this metric.

FCA · UK · Landmark

FCA Mills Review: Seven Recommendations, No New AI Rules — Consumer Duty Holds the Line

Published July 6, the FCA's Mills Review sets out seven priority recommendations for AI in retail financial services through 2030. Key finding: 1 in 5 UK adults is already open to AI making decisions for them; 26% trust ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini for financial advice. The FCA confirms a principles-based approach — Consumer Duty and SM&CR govern AI, with no bespoke AI rules forthcoming.

Why It Matters

Consumer Duty is now officially your product's regulatory anchor. Every AI output your internal analysts act on — or that reaches a retail customer — must be traceable, explainable, and demonstrably in the client's interest. The Mills Review has made that non-negotiable.

Research & Think About
  • Read the FCA Mills Review summary — it's public at fca.org.uk and directly relevant to your product PRDs
  • Under SM&CR, identify who at your firm carries personal accountability for AI-generated advice or outputs
  • Map your product against the FCA's four systemic AI risks: firm operations, consumer journeys, competition, fraud/cyber
