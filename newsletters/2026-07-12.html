Lead Story · AI Portfolio Research

JPMorgan's AI Agents Beat 60/40 — But the Bank Says Don't Trust the Results

Eight AI investing agents built by JPMorgan — powered by models from OpenAI and Anthropic — each outperformed a traditional 60/40 portfolio on a risk-adjusted basis across two decades of backtests. The best topped the 60/40 by 0.7 percentage points annually with lower volatility. But the strategists behind the research were emphatic: "We strongly caution against uncritically accepting what amounts to in-sample, overly confident answers of AI." The agents classified markets into four regimes — Goldilocks, reflation, stagflation, risk-off — and dynamically shifted between stocks and bonds.

Why It Matters

This is the defining tension for AI products in finance: impressive outputs, uncertain reliability. JPMorgan's caution is the same caution your analysts will apply to your product. Evaluation frameworks and honest uncertainty communication are not optional features — they are the product.

Research & Think About
  • Read: "in-sample vs. out-of-sample performance" — understand why backtests routinely overstate real-world returns, and how to explain this to a sceptical portfolio manager
  • Research "market regime classification" — how do quants define macro environments (growth, inflation, risk), and what data signals do they use? This is a core use case for LLMs in finance
  • Ask yourself: how would you design a product that gives analysts AI-generated insight while making model uncertainty visible — not hidden — in the output?

Public Markets 101
Essential concepts for your role

JPMorgan's AI agents classified markets, measured risk-adjusted returns, and evaluated regime performance — all using vocabulary your users take for granted. Six terms that came up directly in this week's biggest story, and that you need working fluency with to build credible products for buy-side analysts.

Sharpe Ratio
A measure of risk-adjusted return: return above a risk-free rate divided by volatility. The primary metric for comparing portfolios of different risk levels. JPMorgan's agents "beat the 60/40 on a risk-adjusted basis" means they scored higher Sharpe Ratios.
Market Capitalisation
Total market value of a company's outstanding shares (share price × shares outstanding). "Large-cap", "mid-cap", "small-cap" refer to this. A key variable in how AI models are trained to classify equities for portfolio allocation.
Beta
A measure of a stock's sensitivity to market moves. Beta of 1 = moves with the market; beta above 1 = amplifies market swings; below 1 = more defensive. AI risk models must correctly estimate beta to give analysts useful portfolio exposure analysis.
Liquidity
How easily an asset can be bought or sold without moving its price. Illiquid assets are harder to exit quickly. AI tools that surface liquidity risk in real time — especially in stress scenarios — give portfolio managers critical early warning that manual analysis often misses.
AUM (Assets Under Management)
The total market value of assets a fund or investment manager oversees on behalf of clients. It determines fee revenue and signals fund scale. LinqAlpha's $5T AUM figure tells you exactly how much capital its signals influence — the scale of the decisions your product affects.
Systematic vs. Discretionary
Systematic strategies use rules, models, and algorithms to trade. Discretionary strategies rely on human judgement. Most large funds blend both. AI is blurring this line — JPMorgan's agents are a systematic layer on top of discretionary macro views.

UK · Principles-Based

UK Regulators Consolidate: FCA, PRA, and Bank of England Stay Technology-Agnostic on AI

The FCA, Prudential Regulation Authority, and Bank of England have each reaffirmed — in separate April 2026 publications — that AI will be governed through existing frameworks (Consumer Duty, SM&CR, operational resilience), not bespoke AI-specific rules. The BoE and PRA explicitly said they are keeping further guardrails "under review."

Why It Matters

"Principles-based" sounds permissive — it isn't. Consumer Duty's "good outcomes" standard applies to every AI output your product produces. The burden of demonstrating compliance sits on your firm, not a rulebook. That makes explainability and audit logging product requirements, not optional extras.

Research & Think About
  • Read the FCA's Consumer Duty guidance — map "good outcome" to specific outputs your product generates. What constitutes a bad outcome, and how would you know if one occurred?
  • Research SM&CR (Senior Managers and Certification Regime) — understand who at your firm is personally accountable if an AI output causes client harm and how that affects your product design
  • Read the Global Policy Watch UK AI Regulation 2026 summary for a concise map of all three regulators' current stances

HBR: To Drive AI Adoption, Build Your Team's Product Management Skills First

A Harvard Business Review piece from February 2026 — now circulating widely in enterprise AI circles — argues that AI adoption failures are primarily product management failures: unclear ownership, no user research, no evaluation criteria, and no feedback loops. The prescription is investing in PM capability before scaling AI deployment, not after.

Why It Matters

This reframes your role from feature-builder to adoption enabler. If analysts aren't using what you ship, the problem is rarely the model. It is almost always discovery, trust, workflow fit, or feedback loops — core PM problems, not engineering ones.

Research & Think About
  • Read the HBR piece at hbr.org — identify the three PM failure modes they describe and map each one to a specific risk in your current product or roadmap
  • Research "user adoption metrics" for internal tools: daily active users, task completion rate, and "would you miss this tool?" are more informative than feature counts or model benchmark scores
  • Write a one-paragraph PM brief: what specific behaviour change would tell you your product is succeeding — not just being used, but changing how an analyst actually works?

How Product Is Changing in 2026: Strategy Is Now the Non-Negotiable PM Skill

Product veteran Ant Murphy argues that AI has removed the tactical grunt work from PM roles — roadmap formatting, note-taking, market scans — and exposed what was always the real job: strategy, narrative, and judgement. 59% of product professionals now cite strategy and business acumen as the most critical skill for the next two to three years, ahead of data literacy and technical knowledge.

Why It Matters

AI is doing the PM admin. What remains is harder, not easier: deciding what to build, for whom, and why — in a domain (public markets) where the stakes for a wrong call are high. Your domain knowledge advantage over generic AI PMs compounds as the tactical work disappears.

Research & Think About
