AI in Public Markets

IPO Watch

OpenAI Files Too — the Trillion-Dollar Trio Is Now Real

A week after Anthropic, OpenAI has filed for its own US IPO, with both expected to go public as soon as the fall — OpenAI reportedly targeting a valuation of up to $1 trillion (versus its $852B March mark and Anthropic's $965B+). Alongside SpaceX, that's three trillion-dollar debuts arriving in quick succession — what Bloomberg calls the most consequential test of investor appetite for high-growth tech in a decade.

Why it matters for you: This stops being a "will they list" story and becomes a "can the market absorb them" story. Three correlated mega-listings in one window is a liquidity-and-sentiment event, not just three tickers. The sharp question for your analysts: if the first debut prices softly, how does that re-rate the other two — and everything held as an AI proxy?

Capital Returns

Nvidia Turns Capital-Returner: $80B Buyback and a 25x Dividend Hike

Fresh off an 85% revenue surge to $81.6B, Nvidia unveiled an $80B share-repurchase program and raised its quarterly dividend 25-fold — from $0.01 to $0.25. Analysts stayed constructive (a "Strong Buy" consensus and a ~$298 average target, roughly 43% above recent levels), but the gesture itself is the signal: a hyper-growth name is now handing cash back.

Why it matters for you: Big buybacks and dividends are what companies do when reinvestment runway starts to look finite relative to cash generation. It doesn't mean growth is over — but it's a subtle regime marker worth tracking. The narrative is maturing from "AI hyper-growth" toward "AI cash machine," and that shift changes which investors own the stock.

Tools for Analysts & PMs

Long Context

Gemini 3.5 Pro's 2M-Token Window: Load the Whole Filing Set at Once

Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro reaches general availability this month with a 2-million-token context window and a "Deep Think" reasoning mode. In practical terms, that's enough to hold an entire S-1, several years of 10-Ks, and the sell-side notes around them in a single prompt — then reason across all of it without the retrieval-stitching that used to fragment the analysis.

Why it matters for you: Long context quietly changes your build-vs-buy math. A chunk of the RAG plumbing you might have scoped — chunking, embedding, re-ranking — gets absorbed by the model. Your differentiation moves to what you put in the window (proprietary data, the right documents) and how you verify what comes out, not the retrieval pipeline itself.

Google

Google Ships Its Agent Stack: Gemini 3.5 Flash, Spark, and a World Model

Out of I/O 2026, Google framed an "agentic Gemini era." Gemini 3.5 Flash now tops the prior 3.1 Pro on hard agentic and coding benchmarks (Terminal-Bench 2.1: 76.2%, MCP Atlas: 83.6%), Gemini Spark is a general-purpose agent that reasons across your connected apps, and Omni is a new world model. Computer Use tooling now ships in the Gemini 3 preview models.

Why it matters for you: The story isn't one model — it's that all three frontier labs (Google, OpenAI, Anthropic) now ship a stack: model + agent + tool-use + connectors. The frontier is shifting from "best model" to "best agent that plugs into real systems." Evaluate vendors on the whole stack and its connectors, not the benchmark headline.

AI Product Management

Model Tiering

Cheap-and-Fast vs. Deep-and-Slow Is Now a Product Decision

The frontier is splitting into tiers: fast, cheap models (Gemini 3.5 Flash) tuned for high-volume agentic work, and deliberate reasoning models (Gemini 3.5 Pro's "Deep Think," OpenAI's o-series) that trade speed and cost for accuracy on hard problems. Efficiency gains mean yesterday's frontier quality now runs at a fraction of the price.

Why it matters for you: Picking "the best model" is the wrong frame — routing is the product. The mature design sends routine, high-volume queries to a cheap fast tier and escalates only the genuinely hard ones to an expensive reasoning tier. Get that routing right and you cut cost an order of magnitude without users noticing; get it wrong and you either bankrupt the feature or make it feel slow.

Regulation

The EU Just Deferred the AI Rules Your Compliance Team Was Bracing For

Under the AI Act Omnibus agreement reached on 7 May, the EU deferred the high-risk obligations most firms were preparing for this August — pushing Annex III use-based requirements (credit scoring, insurance underwriting, recruitment, and more) out to 2 December 2027. The Act still becomes broadly applicable on 2 August 2026, and penalties remain steep: up to €30M or 6% of global turnover. For finance, most credit, underwriting, AML, and fraud systems sit in the high-risk bucket.

Why it matters for you: A deferral is a double-edged gift. It buys breathing room, but the requirements (transparency, human oversight, documentation) are coming regardless — and teams that treat the delay as "later" will scramble in 2027. Build the audit trail and oversight hooks into your product now, while it's cheap, rather than retrofitting them under a deadline.
