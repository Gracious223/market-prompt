AI in Public Markets

Going Public

OpenAI Joins the Queue to Go Public — Now It's Three Giants at Once

Last week, Anthropic (the maker of the Claude chatbot) filed the paperwork to sell shares to the public. Now OpenAI (the maker of ChatGPT) is doing the same. Both could start trading on the stock market — what people call "going public" — as soon as this autumn, and OpenAI is hoping to be valued at up to $1 trillion. Add in Elon Musk's SpaceX, and that's three enormous companies hitting the market in a short window.

Why this matters to you: Until now this was a "will they?" story. Now it's a "can the market handle all three?" story. When several huge companies sell shares at the same time, they compete for the same pool of investor money — so if the first one sells for less than hoped, it can drag down how people value the other two. That's the question your analysts will be asking.

Cash Back

Nvidia Starts Handing Cash Back: an $80B Buyback and a Much Bigger Dividend

Nvidia — the company whose chips power most AI — just had another huge quarter, with sales up 85%. But instead of only reinvesting that money, it's now returning some to shareholders. It announced an $80 billion "buyback" (using spare cash to buy back its own shares, which usually nudges the share price up) and raised its dividend — a regular cash payout to shareholders — 25-fold, from one cent to 25 cents a share.

Why this matters to you: Companies usually start handing cash back when they're making more than they can easily reinvest. It doesn't mean the growth is over — but it's a quiet sign the story is shifting from "explosive growth" to "steady cash machine." And that shift changes the kind of investor who wants to own the stock.

Tools for Analysts & PMs

Handy New Tool

Google's New Gemini Can Read About 15 Novels at Once

Google's new Gemini 3.5 Pro can take in up to 2 million "tokens" — roughly 1.5 million words, or about 15 novels — in a single go, and it has a "Deep Think" mode for harder questions. In plain terms: you could hand it a company's entire stock-market filing, several years of annual reports, and all the analyst notes at the same time, and ask it to make sense of the whole pile at once.

Why this matters to you: Until now, feeding an AI lots of documents meant building a fiddly system that chops them into pieces and fetches the right bits when needed. The model now does much of that work itself. So if you're building a tool, the value isn't in that plumbing anymore — it's in giving the AI the right private documents and carefully checking that its answers are correct.

From the Labs

Google

Google Shows Off Its Full AI Toolkit: a Faster Model, an Assistant, and More

At its big developer event, Google leaned into AI "agents" — software that can take actions for you, not just chat back. Its faster, cheaper model (Gemini 3.5 Flash) now beats last year's top model on tough tests. It also showed Spark, a general assistant that can work across the apps you've connected it to, and Omni, a model that learns how the physical world behaves.

Why this matters to you: All three big AI labs — Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic — now sell a whole bundle: the model, an assistant built on top of it, and the connectors that plug into your other software. So when you choose a provider, judge the whole bundle and how well it links up with your systems — not just which model scores highest on a test.

AI Product Management

Smart Choices

Pick the Right AI for the Job: Cheap-and-Fast vs. Slow-and-Smart

AI models now come in two broad flavours: quick, cheap ones that are great for everyday tasks, and slower, pricier ones that "think harder" to get tough problems right. And because the technology keeps improving, what counted as top-of-the-line last year now costs a fraction of the price to run.

Why this matters to you: "Which model is best?" is the wrong question. The smart move is to send easy, everyday requests to the cheap fast model, and only pass the genuinely hard ones to the expensive "thinking" model. Get that split right and you can cut your costs dramatically without users ever noticing. Get it wrong and you'll either burn money or make the product feel slow.

Watch Closely

New Rules

The EU Just Pushed Back the AI Rules Banks Were Rushing to Meet

The EU's big new AI law was about to put strict rules on "high-risk" uses — things like deciding who gets a loan or what they pay for insurance — starting this August. But lawmakers just delayed those specific rules to December 2027. The wider law still kicks in this August, and the penalties are serious: fines of up to €30 million, or 6% of a company's worldwide sales, whichever is bigger.

Why this matters to you: A delay sounds like good news, but it's a trap if you treat it as "forget about it." The requirements — being open about how the AI works, keeping a human in the loop, and documenting decisions — are still coming. Teams that build those habits in now, while it's cheap and easy, will sail through; teams that wait will be scrambling in 2027.
