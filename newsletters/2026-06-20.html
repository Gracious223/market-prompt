Public Markets

Lead Story · IPO

Anthropic and OpenAI Are Racing to the Same Autumn IPO Window — at a Combined $1.8 Trillion

Anthropic confidentially filed its S-1 (the document a company submits to the US Securities and Exchange Commission — the main financial regulator — before selling shares to the public for the first time) on June 1, at a private valuation of $965 billion — making it the highest-valued AI safety company ever to file for a public listing. Its annualised revenue run rate had crossed $47 billion by May 2026, driven by Claude, Claude Code, and enterprise AI tools. OpenAI followed on June 8, targeting a late-2026 debut at a valuation of up to $852 billion, with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley named as underwriters (the banks that set the opening share price and manage the sale) for both offerings. Both companies are now on course to list in the same autumn window, forcing institutional investors — the pension funds and asset managers that buy large blocks of shares — to choose between two structurally very different businesses at the same time.

Anthropic has fast-growing enterprise revenue and an AI safety brand; OpenAI has massive consumer scale but is burning cash at a rate no software company in history has matched. Pricing both correctly will test every AI investment thesis on Wall Street simultaneously. The ongoing Fable 5 and Mythos 5 suspension makes Anthropic's risk profile harder to read heading into its roadshow — eight days of its two most capable models being offline is a material fact that will appear in every investor's due-diligence file.

IPO Risk · Model Suspension

Fable 5 and Mythos 5 Have Been Offline for Eight Days — and Today Is the Subscriber Refund Deadline

Anthropic launched Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on June 9 to strong enterprise and developer reviews. Three days later, on June 12, a US government export-control directive (a legal order restricting who can access sensitive technology) forced the company to disable both models for every user worldwide — including Anthropic's own foreign national employees. The government's stated concern was a method someone had found to bypass Fable 5's safety guardrails and unlock advanced cybersecurity capabilities. Anthropic complied but publicly stated it disagrees with the action, calling the jailbreak (the technique used to bypass an AI's built-in limits) "a narrow one" that "would not unlock a universal capability." As of today — day eight — both models remain offline. Subscribers who paid for access between June 9 and June 14 are entitled to a refund, and today is that deadline.

An AI company heading into the largest IPO in the sector's history with its two most capable models suspended for eight days is a material risk (a risk large enough to affect an investor's decision) that will be scrutinised line by line when Anthropic's public S-1 filing lands. If the suspension extends into July, it starts to affect quarterly revenue and enterprise contract retention — the exact metrics institutional investors will model first.

Regulation

AI Policy · G7 · Governance

Amodei and Hassabis Went to the G7 in France to Argue for a US-Led Global AI Coalition — Three Days After the Fable 5 Ban

Dario Amodei (Anthropic) and Demis Hassabis (Google DeepMind) joined Sam Altman (OpenAI) at the G7 summit in Évian-les-Bains, France on June 17 — just five days after the US export-control order suspended Fable 5 and Mythos 5. At a closed-door lunch with G7 leaders and President Trump, Amodei advocated for two specific policies: structured access frameworks (rules governing which countries and companies can use the most powerful US AI models) and chip trade agreements that explicitly exclude China. Altman called for "an international forum for discussion that establishes globally accepted standards for testing." G7 leaders discussed a "trusted partners" scheme that would restore allied countries' access to restricted US AI models under a shared governance framework. No binding agreement was reached, but the meeting produced a joint communiqué (a formal statement issued after a diplomatic meeting) on AI safety cooperation.

The G7 meeting is the first time AI lab CEOs have been formally included in a G7 working session — a signal that frontier AI is now treated as strategic infrastructure on par with defence and energy. For investors, the "trusted partners" scheme is the key variable: if it succeeds, it reopens Anthropic's global market and removes the single biggest risk hanging over its IPO roadshow; if it stalls, export restrictions become a permanent feature of the AI competitive landscape.

Watch Closely

Cybersecurity · Geopolitics

150+ Security Experts Say the Fable 5 Ban Backfires — and Zhipu AI's Stock Jumped 33% to Prove It

More than 150 cybersecurity professionals signed an open letter on June 15 urging US Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and National Cyber Director Sean Cairncross to reverse the Fable 5 and Mythos 5 ban. Signatories include Alex Stamos (former chief security officer at Facebook and Yahoo), Katie Moussouris (CEO of Luta Security), Rachel Tobac (of SocialProof Security), and Joe Levy (CEO of Sophos, a major cybersecurity company). Their core argument: the capabilities that triggered the export-control order are not unique to Fable 5 — the same results can be achieved with GPT-5.5, OpenAI Opus, Sonnet, or China's Kimi 2.7, meaning the ban removes tools from US defenders without restricting any adversary. The geopolitical consequence arrived the same week: Chinese AI company Zhipu AI launched its GLM-5.2 model and explicitly cited US AI unreliability in its marketing. Zhipu's stock closed up 33% on June 15, reaching a record high since its listing.

Zhipu's 33% single-day move is the market's real-time verdict on US export controls: if US AI models are unreliable for global buyers, non-US alternatives gain share fast, and the speed of that repricing — within the same week as the ban — should focus any investor modelling AI platform risk. For AI product managers, the open letter's technical argument is equally important: "comparable capabilities from other models" is now a documented fact, not speculation, which changes every procurement conversation about AI vendor lock-in.

From the Labs

Anthropic Alignment · AI Safety

No New Alignment Science Posts — But the Fable 5 Jailbreak Is the Field's Biggest Live Case Study

The Anthropic Alignment Science blog (alignment.anthropic.com) has not published a new post since the SLEIGHT-Bench paper in May, which found that human attackers can bypass AI safety monitors roughly one-in-four times (covered in our June 18 issue). That finding has unexpected real-world relevance this week: the directive suspending Fable 5 was triggered by a jailbreak discovered by a US government security testing partner — the exact category of adversarial attack SLEIGHT-Bench was designed to measure. Anthropic describes the jailbreak as "a narrow one that would not unlock a universal capability." The government describes it as a national security risk. The gap between those two characterisations is the central vocabulary problem alignment researchers must resolve before regulators and regulated institutions can write enforceable AI safety standards. There are no new posts to report today; we check this page daily and will cover any new research here immediately.

The same researchers studying theoretical blind spots in AI safety systems are now managing a real-world case where a theoretical jailbreak triggered an unprecedented regulatory shutdown. For banks, healthcare systems, and defence contractors writing AI procurement policies, the Fable 5 situation is the first empirical data point on what happens when a government decides a jailbreak crosses the national security threshold — and there is still no agreed framework for making that call.

Tools for Analysts

Perplexity Finance Now Pulls from 40+ Live Data Sources — Including the SEC, FactSet, and S&P Global

Perplexity Finance — the AI research assistant that works like a search engine for financial data — has expanded its live data connections to more than 40 sources. They include SEC EDGAR (the US regulator's database of public company filings), FactSet, S&P Global, LSEG (formerly Refinitiv, one of the world's largest financial data providers), Coinbase, Morningstar, Quartr (for live earnings call transcripts), and Polymarket (a prediction market). Users can now ask plain-English questions — "How does Nvidia's gross margin compare to AMD and Intel over the last four quarters?" — and receive sourced answers drawing on live data, without a Bloomberg Terminal (the professional data system most Wall Street analysts use, which costs roughly $25,000 per user per year). Perplexity is on annualised revenue past $450 million and has told CNBC it plans to stay private through 2028 regardless of how the OpenAI and Anthropic IPOs are received.

Institutional-quality financial data has historically been gated behind expensive subscriptions small teams cannot afford. A tool that opens it up via plain-language queries changes who can do Wall Street-calibre research. For AI product managers building analyst tools, Perplexity Finance is now the benchmark to beat: any new analyst AI product needs a clear answer to "why is this better than just asking Perplexity?"

Product Strategy

The Fable 5 Outage Is the Case Study Every AI PM Needed — Build Multi-Provider or Accept the Risk

The Fable 5 and Mythos 5 suspension arrived with no warning at 5:21 PM on a Thursday and is now in its eighth consecutive day with no confirmed end date. For any product team whose application was built exclusively on those models, those eight days meant zero functionality and no clear recovery path. The lesson is architectural: a multi-provider AI gateway — a routing layer that sits between your product and multiple AI model APIs (the programming interfaces that let software connect to AI systems) — eliminates single-provider dependency. The pattern is practical: use one primary model for quality, a fallback from a different provider for availability, and design your prompts (the instructions you send to the AI) to work reasonably well across both. The security experts' open letter this week made the technical case explicit: GPT-5.5 and other models offer comparable capabilities — which means switching costs are lower than many teams assumed.

The Fable 5 outage is the first major real-world test of AI infrastructure resilience, and it arrived with no warning and no certain end date. If your product carries an SLA — a service-level agreement, a written commitment about uptime you make to enterprise customers — a single-model dependency is now a contractual liability you will be asked to explain in every procurement review.
