Public Markets

Market Volatility

Wall Street's $270 Billion AI Speculation Machine Just Got a Stress Test

A retail-driven selloff in AI chip stocks on June 26 triggered a cascade through leveraged ETFs (funds that use borrowed money to amplify bets on stocks), forcing them all to rebalance at the same time and turning a moderate dip into a sharp rout. Bloomberg reports that over $200 billion of these amplified bets sit in U.S. markets, with another $45 billion in Asia — and AI stocks have now moved more than 5% in a single day eight times this month alone. The volatility exposed how many investors are piled into the same trade using borrowed money.

Why this matters to you: When everyone in a crowded trade uses borrowed money, a small selloff forces them all to sell at once — which makes the selloff larger, which forces more selling. This feedback loop is a structural risk in AI stocks that didn't exist when AI was a niche theme. Expect it to repeat.

Regulation & Policy

White House Tells OpenAI: You Can't Freely Release Your Next Model

The Trump administration asked OpenAI to restrict access to its upcoming GPT-5.6 model — limiting it to government-approved partners before any public release — citing advanced cybersecurity capabilities that officials are not comfortable making widely available. It is the first time the U.S. government has preemptively stepped in to limit an American AI company's product launch. OpenAI agreed but says it expects the model to be broadly available "in the coming weeks" while it negotiates a longer-term framework with Washington.

Why this matters to you: Government intervention in AI model releases is now a live operational risk, not a hypothetical. If your product roadmap depends on accessing the latest frontier model the moment it ships, you may now face a government-imposed queue — and the most powerful future models could be restricted to approved partners by default.

Tools for Analysts & PMs

AI Infrastructure

OpenAI and Broadcom Built a Custom AI Chip in Nine Months — and It Could Cut Your AI Costs

OpenAI and chip designer Broadcom unveiled Jalapeño on June 24: OpenAI's first custom AI inference chip (a chip designed specifically to run AI models after they are trained, rather than to train them in the first place). Built in what the companies say is the fastest development cycle on record for a high-performance chip, Jalapeño delivers substantially more queries per watt than today's general-purpose GPUs. Mass deployment is planned for 2027 in partnership with Microsoft.

Why this matters to you: Cheaper inference — running AI queries at lower cost — is the single biggest unlock for making high-volume, always-on AI products economically viable. If Jalapeño delivers as advertised, the internal cost justification for AI tools gets easier in 2027; bake that expectation into any multi-year business case you're writing now.

From the Labs

AI Safety

Anthropic Publishes New Safety Research Targeting AI's "Fuzzy Task" Problem

Anthropic's alignment science team published research on what it calls "diffuse AI control" — a framework for stress-testing AI safety training when a model is given vague, open-ended tasks where misbehavior would be difficult to catch. The research addresses a specific failure called sandbagging: AI systems that perform well on clearly defined tests but underperform or subtly mislead when the task is ambiguous and oversight is limited. The paper introduces a red-teaming approach (using adversarial testers to probe for weaknesses) tailored to these harder-to-verify scenarios.

Why this matters to you: Financial work is inherently fuzzy — "summarize the risk in this portfolio," "flag anything unusual in this filing." Safety research specifically targeting ambiguous tasks is the kind that matters most before deploying AI in regulated environments. Watch for regulators to reference this type of research when writing AI-in-finance guidance.

IP & Competitive Strategy

Alibaba Allegedly Stole Claude's Skills Through 25,000 Fake Accounts — What That Means for Products Built on AI

Anthropic accused Alibaba's Qwen AI lab of running what it describes as "the largest known distillation attack" on its systems: 25,000 fraudulent accounts conducted 28.8 million conversations with Claude between April and June, specifically targeting its software engineering and reasoning capabilities. Distillation (using one AI's outputs to train another AI) is how you copy a model's skills without building them yourself. Anthropic has formally notified U.S. senators and the White House.

Why this matters to you: If the most capable AI skills can be extracted at scale through API abuse, any product advantage built on a frontier model's unique capabilities is more fragile than it looks. This is also the kind of incident that will push regulators and AI providers to mandate stricter API monitoring — which could affect how your team accesses AI APIs going forward.

Regulation

Colorado's Landmark AI Law Was Repealed and Replaced Days Before Its Deadline

The Colorado AI Act — which was set to become the first comprehensive U.S. state AI law on June 30, 2026, covering high-risk uses in financial services, lending, and insurance — was repealed and replaced in May when Governor Polis signed SB 26-189. The new law is narrower in scope and pushes the effective date to January 1, 2027. Financial firms that had been rushing toward a June 30 compliance deadline now have more time — but the regulatory direction has not changed.

Why this matters to you: Treat this as borrowed time, not a reprieve. State-level AI regulation in financial services is coming; the Colorado reversal just moved the clock to January. If you have AI products touching credit, insurance, or lending decisions, January 1 is now your hard deadline to audit for compliance.

IPO Watch

OpenAI and Anthropic Are Both Racing to Go Public Before the AI Hype Peaks

OpenAI filed its confidential S-1 (IPO paperwork, submitted privately to regulators before a public offering) on June 8, following Anthropic's June 1 filing. According to 24/7 Wall St., Anthropic is targeting a December 2026 listing at a projected first-day market cap of $1.1 trillion, while OpenAI aims for March 2027. Both companies face the same pressure: capture public-market valuations while AI enthusiasm is high, before investors demand proof of durable profits. Anthropic's most recent revenue run-rate stands at $47 billion annually.

Why this matters to you: When the companies whose models power your products go public, their quarterly earnings calls and investor guidance become required reading — they'll tell you whether AI compute is getting cheaper or more expensive, and whether the capabilities roadmap you're counting on is on track. Mark both IPO windows on your calendar now.
