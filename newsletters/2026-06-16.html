Lead Story

Anthropic-Trump Stalemate: No Breakthrough After Monday Talks, Fable 5 Ban Holds

Senior technical staff from Anthropic met with Commerce Department officials on Monday, June 15, to discuss the Fable 5 and Mythos 5 export ban — but the sides remain split. The administration told Fox Business that Anthropic's "recklessness" created a lack of trust; Anthropic says it received only "verbal evidence of a potential narrow jailbreak" and disagrees that a limited finding justifies pulling commercial models used by hundreds of millions of people. A spokesperson said both sides are "working quickly to resolve" the dispute, but the export ban is still in force today, day four.

Anthropic filed a confidential S-1 (the paperwork companies submit before going public) on 1 June at a $965 billion target valuation. Every day the ban continues is a day that headline risk sits directly on the IPO story — and reminds investors that model access can be revoked overnight by government directive, not just vendor policy.

Index Mechanics

Nasdaq-100 Adds CoreWeave and Nebius on June 22 — $800B in Passive Funds Must Buy Both

Nasdaq's June quarterly rebalance, announced June 12, adds five companies to the Nasdaq-100 index (an index of the 100 largest non-financial companies on Nasdaq): CoreWeave (cloud AI computing), Nebius Group (AI cloud infrastructure), Astera Labs (networking chips), Rocket Lab, and Teradyne. The change takes effect before market open on June 22. Every ETF (exchange-traded fund) and index fund that tracks the Nasdaq-100 — including the $800B-plus QQQ fund — must buy shares in each new entrant to match the index, regardless of price. Nebius is up 163% year-to-date; CoreWeave carries $14B in debt.

Three of the five additions are pure-play AI infrastructure names. When passive funds are forced to buy a stock, the buying pressure is mechanical and indiscriminate — it is not a vote on whether the company is fairly valued, which is exactly the kind of distinction your product should surface for portfolio managers (PMs who manage investment portfolios) evaluating AI names this week.

Databricks Summit Day 2: Lakebase Goes Live — A Transactional Database Built Into the AI Stack

Day two of the Databricks Data + AI Summit in San Francisco brings the formal launch of Lakebase, a new operational (real-time, read-write) database that sits inside Databricks' existing data platform. Unlike a traditional data warehouse — which stores historical data for analysis — Lakebase handles live transactions, letting AI agents read and write to the same data store your analytics team already uses. Financial services sessions running today cover AI in risk, underwriting, and compliance. Anthropic, Google, AWS, and Deloitte are all presenting on the floor.

Most AI pilots fail because the agent can't access the live data it needs — it's stuck with a snapshot. A transactional database built natively into the AI platform is the infrastructure change that makes real-time agentic workflows in finance feasible, not just a demo.

EU AI Act · Europe

EU Grants a 16-Month Reprieve on High-Risk AI — Compliance Deadline Moves to December 2027

On May 7, the European Council and Parliament reached a provisional agreement under the "Digital Omnibus" package to delay the EU AI Act's high-risk AI obligations. Companies whose AI systems fall into Annex III (use-based risks — think AI in hiring, credit scoring, and biometric surveillance) now have until December 2, 2027 instead of August 2, 2026. Product-regulated systems get an extra year, to August 2028. The delay gives standards bodies — the organisations that write the technical rules companies must follow — more time to produce the guidance that firms are waiting on before they can actually comply.

If your product touches EU customers and uses AI in a consequential decision (credit, employment, insurance), the 16-month extension changes your compliance roadmap — but does not remove the obligation. Use the time to build the documentation and audit trail the Act will require, rather than treating the delay as a pause.

From the Labs

OpenAI

OpenAI's 2025 Finances Laid Bare: $34B Spent, $39B in Losses — The IPO Math Is Brutal

The Financial Times obtained OpenAI's audited 2025 financials ahead of the company's planned IPO (initial public offering — the first time a company sells shares to the public). Total spending hit $34 billion: $19B on research and development, nearly $6B on sales and marketing, with the rest on infrastructure and operations. Revenue reached $13 billion — meaning the company burned roughly $2.60 for every dollar it earned. The net loss widened from $5B in 2024 to $39B in 2025, though most of that jump reflects non-cash restructuring charges; the operational loss was $8B.

OpenAI is pursuing a valuation rumoured above $200B despite losing $8B a year on an operational basis — a bet that revenue growth justifies the price. Your analysts need a framework for valuing a company with no profitable precedent: the question is whether this is Amazon circa 2001 (burn now, dominate later) or a story that never converts spending into earnings.

Model Race

June's Model Pipeline: Gemini 3.5 Pro Still Undelivered, Grok 5 at 33% Odds, Claude Sonnet 4.8 Expected

Google announced at I/O in May that Gemini 3.5 Pro would arrive "next month" — it has not shipped yet as of today. Grok 5 from xAI (Elon Musk's AI company) is still training on Colossus 2; prediction markets put the odds of a June 30 release at just 33%. Claude Sonnet 4.8 from Anthropic is expected before month end. Claude Mythos 1, Anthropic's most capable research model, remains restricted to 50 security-research partners and is not publicly available — separate from the Fable 5 export control situation. GPT-5.5 received a quiet update adding improved structured outputs.

The gap between "announced" and "shipped" is widening for every lab this month. If your product roadmap assumes a specific model's capabilities will be available by a certain date, treat all current announcements as aspirational until the model is in your API (the technical connection your software uses to call an AI model).

Infrastructure Bets

Applied Digital's $5.2B, 30-Year AI Data Center Lease: What Generational Infrastructure Commitments Mean for Your Roadmap

Applied Digital (APLD), a US AI data-center operator, signed a 15-year lease worth $5.2 billion with an undisclosed US hyperscaler (the term for the largest cloud providers: Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Oracle) for 210 megawatts of AI computing capacity at its Delta Forge 2 campus in the Southern US. Including renewal options, the contract could reach $12.7 billion over 30 years. The announcement, made June 9, sent the stock up more than 10%. Applied Digital now holds $36 billion in contracted long-term revenue across five AI campuses.

Hyperscalers are making 15- to 30-year infrastructure commitments to secure AI computing capacity — locking in compute supply the same way airlines lock in jet fuel. As an AI PM, this signals that the vendors you depend on are treating AI infrastructure as a decades-long strategic asset, not a flexible cost line — which means the platform choices you make now carry far longer tail risk than previous technology cycles.
